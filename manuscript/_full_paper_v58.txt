Transcriptomic and Single-Nucleus Transcriptomic Evidence Suggests Dysregulation of an O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-Ferroptosis Regulatory Axis in Myocardial Ischemia-Reperfusion Injury
O-GlcNAc and Ferroptosis in Myocardial I/R
Abstract
Background: Myocardial ischemia-reperfusion injury (MIRI) remains a major clinical challenge, Yang et al. (2025) recently established that O-GlcNAcylation of Nrf2 at threonine 334 regulates ferroptosis through G6PDH in pulmonary epithelial cells. However, this O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis regulatory axis has not been systematically characterized in the heart at multi-scale resolution. Here we present an integrative multi-omics characterization of this axis, with transcriptomic emphasis, in myocardial ischemia-reperfusion injury, combining transcriptomics, proteomics, single-cell transcriptomics, and Boolean network modeling.
Methods: Two bulk RNA-seq datasets (GSE193997, n=24; GSE307385, n=15) were integrated with snRNA-seq data (GSE240848, 48,633 quality-filtered cardiac nuclei (from 48,637 raw nuclei) from rat myocardial I/R) analyzed de novo via scanpy. scRNA-seq data (GSE163129, 33,977 mouse cardiac CD45+ immune cells) were referenced for literature-level cross-validation of immune cell dynamics. Proteomics data from three independent cardiac I/R datasets (PXD046631 LFQ, PXD040135 LFQ, PXD035154 TMT) were analyzed for mRNA-protein concordance, consistently revealing ~50% directional concordance across two quantification platforms. Differential expression (DESeq2), gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA), upstream regulator prediction (Enrichr), Boolean network modeling, weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA), Enrichr-based drug repositioning (DSigDB/LINCS) with molecular docking validation against GPX4 and Keap1, and protein-protein interaction network construction were performed. Raw data deposited in GEO had undergone primary quality control by the original authors prior to download, including empty droplet removal and mitochondrial content filtering.
Results: DESeq2 identified 3,547 up-regulated and 3,230 down-regulated genes (2,461 of which, including 1,368 up-regulated and 1,093 down-regulated, exceeded the |log2FC| > 1 threshold used for volcano plot highlighting) (adjusted P < 0.05) in I/R 6h versus Sham. Among key genes, Slc7a11 was markedly up-regulated (log2FC = +4.07, Padj = 8.09 × 10⁻⁹) alongside Nfe2l2 (log2FC = +0.53, Padj = 1.55 × 10⁻⁴), while Ogt was significantly down-regulated (log2FC = −0.56, Padj = 3.09 × 10⁻⁷). GSEA identified enrichment of ferroptosis-related genes (NES = 1.77, FDR = 0.001) and oxidative stress (GO:0006979, NES = 1.17, FDR = 0.048). NFE2L2 (Nrf2) was predicted as a master upstream transcription factor (ChEA 2022, P = 3.41 × 10⁻⁷). Full single-nucleus RNA-seq analysis of 48,633 quality-filtered cardiac nuclei (GSE240848, from 48,637 raw nuclei, 32,623 genes) with UMAP dimension reduction and Leiden clustering (21 clusters) resolved nine transcriptionally distinct cardiac cell types. Ogt was most highly expressed in cardiomyocytes (52.2% of nuclei positive) and pericytes (47.0%), while Nfe2l2 and its target Hmox1 predominated in macrophages (Nfe2l2: 26.5% positive; Hmox1: 20.2%) and neutrophils (Hmox1: 14.4%). The Nrf2 pathway module score was selectively elevated in neutrophils and macrophages, whereas the O-GlcNAc pathway module score was highest in pericytes and cardiomyocytes, suggesting a cell-type-specific segregation of the O-GlcNAc and Nrf2/ferroptosis transcriptional programs.
 Single-cell resolution analysis revealed cell-type-specific spatial segregation of this axis, with Ogt predominantly expressed in cardiomyocytes and pericytes while ferroptosis markers were enriched in macrophages and neutrophils, suggesting paracrine crosstalk rather than cell-autonomous regulation. Conclusion: Integrative transcriptomic multi-omics evidence from independent transcriptomic datasets at bulk and single-cell resolution, cross-referenced with proteomics (mRNA–protein directional concordance was near-random (50.7%, binomial P = 0.99), consistent with dominant post-transcriptional regulation) and corroborated across three species, extends the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis regulatory axis to the cardiac I/R context through a multi-resolution evidence integration framework for the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network in myocardial I/R injury. This framework yields six experimentally testable hypotheses: (1) transcriptional Ogt suppression is associated with O-GlcNAc erosion during early reperfusion, (2) Nrf2-mediated ferroptosis gene activation represents an adaptive cytoprotective response, (3) O-GlcNAc and Nrf2/ferroptosis pathways are cell-type-segregated between structural and immune compartments, (4) the Boolean regulatory network recapitulates in vivo transcriptional states, (5) GSK3A serves as a candidate kinase linking O-GlcNAc dysregulation to Nrf2 activation, and (6) BACH1 mediates gene-specific Nrf2 target suppression. These predictions establish a computational foundation for the systems-level discovery of pharmacological interventions targeting O-GlcNAc-dependent cardioprotection.
Keywords: myocardial ischemia-reperfusion injury; O-GlcNAc; Ogt; Oga; Nrf2; ferroptosis; transcriptomics; bioinformatics; GEO
1. Introduction
Acute myocardial infarction remains the leading cause of death worldwide, with an estimated 9 million deaths annually [1]. Timely reperfusion via percutaneous coronary intervention or thrombolysis is the standard of care for restoring coronary blood flow to the ischemic myocardium [2]. However, the process of reperfusion itself can paradoxically induce additional myocardial injury—a phenomenon known as myocardial ischemia-reperfusion injury (MIRI)—which accounts for up to 50% of the final infarct size [3,4]. Despite decades of research, no pharmacological intervention has been conclusively demonstrated to prevent MIRI in clinical practice [5,6]. This therapeutic gap underscores the urgent need for a deeper understanding of the molecular mechanisms underlying MIRI.
O-GlcNAcylation is a dynamic and reversible post-translational modification in which a single N-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc) moiety is added to serine or threonine residues of nuclear, cytoplasmic, and mitochondrial proteins [7]. This modification is catalyzed by a single pair of enzymes: O-GlcNAc transferase (OGT), which adds the sugar, and O-GlcNAcase (OGA), which removes it [8]. As a nutrient-responsive modification that integrates glucose, amino acid, and fatty acid metabolism, O-GlcNAcylation functions as a cellular stress sensor [9]. In the cardiovascular system, acute augmentation of O-GlcNAc levels has been shown to confer cardioprotection against ischemia-reperfusion injury by reducing reactive oxygen species, attenuating endoplasmic reticulum stress, and preventing mitochondrial permeability transition pore opening [10-11]. Conversely, sustained elevation of O-GlcNAcylation drives pathological cardiac hypertrophy and heart failure [12,13]. Despite extensive characterization of the cardioprotective role of O-GlcNAc at the protein level, the transcriptional regulation of the O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes (Ogt and Oga) during MIRI remains poorly characterized.
Ferroptosis is a recently discovered form of regulated cell death driven by iron-dependent lipid peroxidation [14]. Unlike apoptosis and necrosis, ferroptosis has distinct morphological, biochemical, and genetic features, and is executed through the failure of glutathione-dependent lipid peroxide repair systems, particularly glutathione peroxidase 4 (GPX4) [15,16]. A growing body of evidence has established that ferroptosis plays a critical role in MIRI [17,18]. The cystine/glutamate antiporter subunit SLC7A11 and GPX4 are key inhibitors of ferroptosis; their suppression sensitizes cardiomyocytes to ferroptotic death during I/R [19]. The transcription factor nuclear factor erythroid 2-related factor 2 (NFE2L2/Nrf2) serves as the master regulator of the antioxidant response, transcriptionally activating a battery of genes including GPX4, SLC7A11, and glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PDX) that collectively suppress ferroptosis [20,21].
Importantly, a recent seminal study by Yang et al. (2025) showed that O-GlcNAcylation of Nrf2 at threonine 334 enhances its transcriptional activity toward G6PDH, thereby protecting pulmonary epithelial cells against ischemia-reperfusion-induced ferroptosis [22]. This discovery established, for the first time, a direct molecular link between O-GlcNAc modification and ferroptosis through the Nrf2 signaling axis. However, whether a similar O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis regulatory mechanism operates in the myocardium—which possesses distinct metabolic characteristics and ferroptosis susceptibility compared to pulmonary tissue—remains unexplored. The present study builds on this mechanistic foundation to provide the first systems-level characterization of the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis axis specifically in the cardiac I/R context.
Addressing this question requires a multi-resolution analytical framework that transcends the limitations of individual omics platforms. Single-platform transcriptomic studies can identify differentially expressed genes but cannot resolve cell-type-specific regulation, verify protein-level concordance, or establish cross-species generalizability—three essential dimensions for understanding a regulatory axis that spans transcriptional, post-translational, and metabolic layers. The O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network operates across multiple cell types within the heart and its components are regulated at both mRNA and protein levels, necessitating integrated analysis at multiple resolutions. Furthermore, the consistency of O-GlcNAc-related transcriptional changes across independent studies, experimental models, and species remains unexamined, representing a critical gap in translating transcriptomic observations into biological understanding.
In the present study, we performed a systematic multi-platform integrative transcriptomic analysis combining four independent myocardial I/R datasets—two bulk RNA-seq datasets (GSE193997 for discovery, n=24; GSE307385 for time-series validation, n=15), a snRNA-seq dataset (GSE240848, 48,633 quality-filtered cardiac nuclei (from 48,637 raw) from rat MIRI) analyzed de novo at full transcriptomic depth, an scRNA-seq dataset (GSE163129, 33,977 mouse cardiac CD45+ immune cells) referenced for cross-referencing, and proteomics data (PXD046631, mouse I/R heart) for exploratory mRNA-protein concordance assessment. Through systematic bioinformatics analysis including differential expression, gene set enrichment, upstream regulator prediction, Boolean network modeling, co-expression network analysis, virtual drug screening, and protein-protein interaction network construction, we provide a cell-type-resolved systems-level characterization of the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network in MIRI, building on the mechanistic framework established by Yang et al. (2025). This is a transcriptomic hypothesis-generating study; all analyses are performed on publicly available datasets, and the conclusions drawn constitute statistical inferences at the transcriptome and proteome level rather than experimental mechanistic validation.
2. Materials and Methods
2.1 Data Acquisition
Two bulk RNA-seq datasets—the primary transcriptomic data sources for this study—were obtained from the NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/).
GSE193997 (Liu H et al., Free Radical Biology and Medicine, 2022; PMID: 35843476): This RNA-seq dataset profiled mRNA expression in wild-type C57BL/6 mouse heart tissue following sham surgery or 45 minutes of ischemia followed by reperfusion for 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 3.0, 6.0, or 12.0 hours (n = 3 per group; 24 samples total). Sequencing was performed on the Illumina HiSeq platform (GPL17021). Raw count data (filteredCounts) for each sample were extracted from the supplementary RAW archive and merged into a unified expression matrix of 46,078 genes. This dataset was used as the primary discovery cohort for differential expression analysis at the 6h reperfusion time point.
GSE307385 (Zhang X et al., 2025; Fudan University): This RNA-seq dataset profiled mRNA expression in wild-type mouse heart tissue (ischemic region) following sham surgery or 30 minutes of ischemia followed by reperfusion for 0, 1, 6, or 24 hours (n = 3 per group; 15 samples total). Sequencing was performed on the HiSeq X Ten platform (GPL21273). FPKM expression values for all 18,072 genes were obtained from the supplementary file `GSE307385_FPKM_allsamples.txt.gz`. This dataset was used as an independent validation cohort for time-series analysis of O-GlcNAc cycling enzyme expression.
Important note on dataset integration: The two datasets were not blindly merged but analyzed as independent replicates with distinct roles. GSE193997 (45 min ischemia) served as the discovery cohort for DEG analysis, while GSE307385 (30 min ischemia) provided independent time-series validation. Protocol differences in ischemia duration and sequencing platform are acknowledged as a limitation (see Discussion 4.8). The consistent direction of Ogt and Oga dysregulation across both datasets, despite these methodological differences, strengthens the evidence for O-GlcNAc dysregulation as a conserved feature of MIRI (see Supplementary Figure S1).

2.1.1 O-GlcNAc Site Mapping and dbOGAP Curation

To systematically map known O-GlcNAc modification sites across the 27 key genes in the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network, we queried the dbOGAP database (https://dbogap.org/) and the UniProt KnowledgeBase (https://www.uniprot.org/) for experimentally validated O-GlcNAc sites in Mus musculus. For each gene, glycosylation annotations were retrieved using UniProt REST API with organism filter (organism_id:10090) and carbohydrate modification field (ft_carbohyd). O-GlcNAc sites were identified from CARBOHYD annotations containing 'O-linked (GlcNAc)' modification records. Additionally, literature-mining was performed to capture sites reported in targeted O-GlcNAc proteomics studies not yet deposited in dbOGAP. The hexosamine biosynthesis pathway (HBP) genes (GFPT1, GFPT2, GNPNAT1, PGM3, UAP1, OGT, OGA, MGEA5) were extracted from the GSE193997 DESeq2 results and their expression changes were visualized as a pathway-level heatmap.
2.1.2 snRNA-seq Data (GSE240848)
For single-cell resolution validation, the GSE240848 snRNA-seq dataset was obtained from the Gene Expression Omnibus. This dataset profiles single-nucleus RNA expression from rat myocardial ischemia-reperfusion tissue (10x Genomics Chromium platform, Cell Ranger 5.0.1). The full matrix containing 32,623 genes across 48,637 nuclei was analyzed using Scanpy (v1.12.1) in Python [41]. Quality control filtered nuclei with fewer than 200 detected genes or >25% mitochondrial reads, retaining 48,633 high-quality nuclei (Supplementary Figure S39) (the low exclusion rate of 0.008% is consistent with the data having been pre-filtered by the GEO submitting authors; GSE240848 represents a quality-controlled subset of the original dataset) and 21,653 genes. Counts were normalized to 10,000 reads per nucleus, log1p-transformed, and the top 2,000 highly variable genes (HVGs) were selected for dimensionality reduction. Principal component analysis (30 PCs) was performed on HVGs, followed by neighborhood graph construction (n_neighbors=15) and UMAP embedding (min_dist=0.3, spread=1.0). Unsupervised Leiden clustering (resolution=0.8) identified 21 transcriptionally distinct clusters. Cell type annotation was performed using marker gene scoring (scanpy.tl.score_genes) against canonical cardiac markers for nine cell types: cardiomyocyte (Tnnt2, Myh7, Actc1), fibroblast (Col1a1, Col1a2, Dcn), endothelial (Pecam1, Cdh5, Kdr), macrophage (Cd68, Csf1r, Adgre1), neutrophil (S100a8, S100a9), T cell (Cd3e, Cd3d), B cell (Cd19, Cd79a), smooth muscle (Acta2, Myh11), and pericyte (Pdgfrb, Rgs5). Pathway module scores for Nrf2 antioxidant (13 genes), ferroptosis (10 genes), and O-GlcNAc (7 genes) were calculated using scanpy.tl.score_genes with default control gene sets.
Cell-Cell Communication Inference. To infer ligand-receptor interactions among the nine cardiac cell types, we implemented a Python-based ligand-receptor co-expression analysis. Communication scores were calculated using a simplified mass-action formulation: for each ligand-receptor (L-R) pair, the communication score from sender cell type A to receiver cell type B was computed as S(L,R) = L_avg × R_avg / (1 + L_avg × R_avg), where L_avg is the mean expression of the ligand in cell type A and R_avg is the mean expression of the receptor in cell type B. This scoring approach is conceptually inspired by the CellChat framework (Jin et al., 2021, Nature Communications) but represents a simplified Python implementation and does not reproduce the full CellChat R package algorithm. A curated database of 110 ligand-receptor pairs (sourced from CellChatDB v2, connectomeDB, and recent cardiac single-cell studies; full list in Supplementary Table S28) spanning 12 functional pathways (Growth Factor, Cytokine, Chemokine, TGFβ, Morphogen, ECM, DAMP, Cell Adhesion, Nrf2/Ferroptosis, O-GlcNAc/HBP, Apoptosis, and Other) was assembled from the CellChatDB mouse database and recent literature on O-GlcNAc and ferroptosis signaling. Ligand-receptor pairs were filtered to retain only those with ≥5% expressing nuclei in both sender and receiver cell types. Raw gene counts from adata.raw were used for expression quantification to avoid distortion from normalization. The resulting 9 × 9 cell-type communication network was analyzed for outgoing and incoming signal strength, pathway dominance, and O-GlcNAc/Nrf2-ferroptosis-specific signaling patterns.
2.1.3 scRNA-seq Reference Data (GSE163129)
For literature-based cross-validation of immune cell dynamics, we referenced the GSE163129 scRNA-seq dataset (Jung et al., Nature Communications, 2022). This dataset profiles CD45+ cardiac immune cells at five time points (steady-state, Days 1, 3, 5, and 7 post-myocardial infarction) in adult mice, comprising 33,977 cells. Cell-type proportions and temporal dynamics reported in the original publication were used as an external reference for immune cell population shifts observed in our snRNA-seq analysis. No re-analysis of raw data was performed for this dataset.
2.1.4 Proteomics Data (PXD046631) [40]
For mRNA–protein concordance assessment, the PXD046631 proteomics dataset was obtained from the PRIDE (Proteomics Identifications) database. This dataset provides lactylome and proteome profiling of mouse I/R heart tissue (MI 30 min + I/R 6 h vs. Sham) using label-free quantitative (LFQ) mass spectrometry, with MaxQuant-processed proteinGroups.txt output. Protein-level differential expression was assessed using Welch’s t-test on log2-transformed LFQ intensities with Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate correction, requiring ≥2 valid LFQ values per group. mRNA–protein correlation was assessed across all 2,308 genes with both mRNA and protein measurements using Spearman correlation and directional concordance analysis. Twenty-six key genes spanning O-GlcNAc cycling, Nrf2 regulation, antioxidant defense, glutathione metabolism, and ferroptosis were highlighted for focused analysis. Protein-level GSEA (fgseaMultilevel) was performed on the protein log2FC ranking to assess pathway-level enrichment of Nrf2-ARE, ferroptosis driver/suppressor, and O-GlcNAc cycling gene sets. Pathway-level concordance was evaluated by comparing mean mRNA and protein log2FC across gene sets. A second proteomics dataset, PXD045759 (mouse cardiac I/R total proteome, TMT 10-plex), was assessed for cross-platform validation but was found to contain only peptide-spectrum match identifications (Comet search engine output) without processed TMT reporter ion quantification, precluding differential expression re-analysis. An alternative dataset, PXD040135 (mouse cardiac I/R, label-free quantification, n=6), was therefore analyzed for independent cross-platform mRNA–protein concordance assessment. Protein-level differential expression in PXD040135 was assessed using the same Welch's t-test and ≥2 valid LFQ value threshold on normalized abundances, and results are presented alongside PXD046631 findings in Section 3.2.1. A third proteomics dataset, PXD035154 (Hofmann et al., Circulation, 2024; mouse cardiac I/R, TMT10plex, n=2–3 per group), was independently re-analyzed to further assess cross-platform generalizability. This dataset applies isobaric TMT labeling (reporter ions at m/z 126–131) and IsobarQuant quantification, providing substantially greater proteomic depth (~6,400–6,500 gene products vs. ~2,308 in PXD046631 and ~999 in PXD040135). PXD035154 includes two independent I/R pools: Pool 1 (2-day I/R, Sham_I/R n=2 vs. I/R_2d n=3) and Pool 2 (2-week I/R, Sham_I/R n=2 vs. I/R_2w n=3). Differential expression was computed from TMT signal_sum intensities using Welch's t-test without multiple-testing correction, consistent with the PXD046631 and PXD040135 analyses. Ensembl gene IDs were mapped to gene symbols via the mygene Python package (MyGeneInfo API), and protein groups were merged by gene symbol using the maximum total signal. mRNA–protein directional concordance and Spearman correlation were assessed for 27 key network genes using mRNA log2FC from GSE193997; results are presented alongside PXD046631 and PXD040135 findings in Section 3.2.1.
2.2 Differential Expression Analysis
For GSE193997, the comparison between Sham (n=3) and I/R 6h (n=3) was selected as the primary analysis, as the 6-hour time point is the most widely used endpoint in MIRI transcriptomic studies and corresponds to the peak of acute inflammatory and cell death responses [18,23]. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified using DESeq2 (v1.46.0) [24] in R (v4.6.0). Genes with fewer than 10 counts in at least 3 samples were filtered out prior to analysis. The Benjamini-Hochberg procedure was applied for multiple testing correction, with an adjusted P-value (P_adj) threshold of 0.05. ENSEMBL gene identifiers were mapped to gene symbols using org.Mm.eg.db (v3.20.0). DESeq2 quality control included inspection of the dispersion-vs-mean plot (Supplementary Figure S21), MA plots with apeglm log-fold-change shrinkage (Supplementary Figure S22), and sample-to-sample distance heatmaps (Supplementary Figure S30) to verify the absence of outlier samples. Gene-level Cook's distances were examined to identify potentially influential observations. Given the in vivo nature of the I/R model with inherently high biological variability, DEGs were defined solely by adjusted P-value < 0.05 without imposing an additional fold-change threshold, as the power analysis confirmed adequate sensitivity to detect biologically meaningful changes at the available sample size (n = 3 per group; Methods 2.9). For the independent validation cohort (GSE307385), pre-computed FPKM values were used as provided by the data depositor without re-processing from raw counts; as a validation rather than discovery dataset, descriptive FPKM-based analyses (fold-change computation and time-series visualization) are appropriate for corroborating expression trends identified in the discovery cohort (GSE193997) where formal statistical testing was performed using count-based DESeq2.
2.3 Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA)
Genes were ranked by log_2 fold change (Sham vs. I/R 6h) and analyzed using the GSEA algorithm implemented in clusterProfiler (v4.20.0) [25].
GO Biological Process GSEA: Gene Ontology Biological Process enrichment was performed using `gseGO()` with the org.Mm.eg.db database and default parameters (minGSSize = 10, maxGSSize = 500, pvalueCutoff = 0.25).
Custom Nrf2-ARE/Ferroptosis Gene Set: A manually curated gene set comprising 47 genes (43 detected in the expression-ranked gene list) was constructed based on established literature [20,21,26,27], including: (i) classical Nrf2 target genes (Gclc, Gclm, Gsr, Txnrd1, Prdx1, Sod1, Sod2, Cat, Hmox1, Nqo1, Txn1, Srxn1); (ii) glutathione metabolism enzymes (Gsta1-4, Gstm1-5, Gstp1, Gstt1-2); (iii) ferroptosis core genes (Gpx4, Slc7a11, Fth1, Ftl1, Tfrc, Acsl4, Ptgs2); (iv) Nrf2 regulatory factors (Sqstm1, Keap1, Nfe2l2); and (v) NADPH/redox enzymes (G6pdx, Pgd). GSEA was performed using `GSEA()` with this custom gene set (minGSSize = 5, maxGSSize = 500, pvalueCutoff = 0.5). A random permutation test (10,000 iterations, see section 2.4) was used to determine whether the observed enrichment was greater than expected by chance. A competitive gene set test was performed by comparing the observed NES against the NES distribution of 1,000 randomly sampled gene sets of equal size. KEGG pathway GSEA was performed using `gseKEGG()` with organism="mmu" via the KEGG API.
2.4 Upstream Regulator Prediction
To identify transcription factors (TFs) and kinases potentially driving the observed transcriptional changes, the top 500 up-regulated and 500 down-regulated DEGs (ranked by |log_2FC|, P_adj < 0.05) were submitted to Enrichr (https://maayanlab.cloud/Enrichr/) [28]. TF enrichment was performed against the ChEA 2022 database (ChIP-seq enrichment analysis) and the ENCODE/ChEA Consensus TFs database. Kinase enrichment was performed against the KEA 2015 database (kinase enrichment analysis). Significance was assessed using the Fisher's exact test with Benjamini-Hochberg correction.
Permutation test for NFE2L2 target gene enrichment: To rule out the possibility that the observed NFE2L2 enrichment in ChEA was driven by chance, a random permutation test was performed. Gene labels were randomly shuffled 10,000 times, and the ChEA enrichment P-value was recomputed for each permutation using the same Enrichr pipeline. The empirical P-value was calculated as the fraction of permutations yielding an enrichment score equal to or more significant than the observed value (P = 4.01×10^−8), confirming that NFE2L2 enrichment was not attributable to random DEG composition (empirical P < 0.01). The background gene set comprised all 13,522 expressed genes retained after filtering.
2.5 Enrichr-Based Drug Repositioning with Molecular Docking Validation
To identify drugs and compounds capable of reversing the I/R gene expression signature, DEGs were queried against the Drug Signatures Database (DSigDB), DrugMatrix, and LINCS L1000 Chemical Perturbation databases via Enrichr [28]. The rationale was that drugs whose gene targets significantly overlap with the I/R-up-regulated DEGs—and thus may down-regulate these genes—represent compounds warranting further investigation. Similarly, drugs whose targets overlap with I/R-down-regulated DEGs may restore protective gene expression.
2.5.1 Cross-Database Rank Aggregation

To integrate drug screening results across databases, rank aggregation was performed on the top 50 drugs from DSigDB, DrugMatrix, and LINCS L1000 databases. Drug names were standardized across databases (including synonym resolution and case normalization), and Fisher's method was used to combine P-values from multiple databases for each unique drug. Drugs appearing in ≥2 databases with significant combined scores were classified as high-confidence candidates. An UpSet plot was generated to visualize the intersection patterns of top-ranked drugs across the three databases.
2.5.2 Molecular Docking Validation

To provide orthogonal structure-based validation of the Enrichr-derived repositioning candidates, molecular docking was performed for the top three cross-database-consensus compounds (AZD-7762, dasatinib, and valsartan) against two ferroptosis-related protein targets: glutathione peroxidase 4 (GPX4; PDB: 6HKQ, 1.54 Å resolution, co-crystallized with RSL3) and Kelch-like ECH-associated protein 1 (Keap1; PDB: 5FNQ, 1.91 Å resolution, co-crystallized with a small-molecule inhibitor. RSL3 was selected as the positive control for GPX4 docking because it is a covalent inhibitor of GPX4 (IC50 ≈ 0.1 μM) co-crystallized in the 6HKQ structure used for docking. The Nrf2 9-mer peptide (DEETGE-motif; Kd ≈ 20 nM) was used as the crystallographic reference ligand for the Keap1 Kelch domain (PDB: 5FNQ) (S0W, 3-(4-chlorophenyl)propanoic acid)). Receptor structures were prepared by removing water molecules and non-cognate ligands, adding polar hydrogens, and assigning Gasteiger partial charges. Three-dimensional conformers of each ligand were generated using ETKDG v3 [53] and energy-minimized with the MMFF94 force field [54] in RDKit [52]. Molecular docking was performed with AutoDock Vina 1.2.7 [55] using an exhaustiveness of 16 and generating nine binding poses per ligand. The docking grid (20 × 20 × 20 Å³ for GPX4; 20 × 20 × 20 Å³ for Keap1) was centered on the co-crystallized ligand binding site. Protocol validity was confirmed by re-docking the native ligand RSL3 into the GPX4 binding pocket (RMSD < 2.0 Å between docked and crystallographic poses). RSL3 (PubChem CID: 86289005) served as a positive control for GPX4, and the co-crystallized ligand S0W served as the positive control for Keap1. We note that AutoDock Vina binding energies are approximate and do not account for protein flexibility, solvation effects, or entropic contributions. Accordingly, docking scores are used exclusively for relative ranking within each target, not as absolute binding affinity predictions.
2.6 Time-Series Analysis (GSE307385)

2.6.1 WGCNA Co-expression Network Analysis
To identify co-expression modules associated with myocardial I/R injury, weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA) was performed on the GSE193997 time-series dataset (24 samples: Sham, Ischemia, and I/R at 0.5h/1h/1.5h/3h/6h/12h; n=3 each). Genes with counts-per-million (CPM) > 1 in at least three samples were retained (14,774 genes), and the top 4,000 most variable genes were selected for network construction. A signed adjacency matrix was constructed using the soft-thresholding power β = 3 (scale-free topology fit R² = 0.935). The topological overlap matrix (TOM) was computed to quantify inter-gene connection similarity, and hierarchical clustering (average linkage) of the TOM-based dissimilarity (1 − TOM) was performed. Co-expression modules were identified by adaptive tree cutting (dynamic hybrid cut), with modules containing fewer than 15 genes reassigned to the grey (unassigned) module. Module eigengenes (first principal component of scaled expression) were computed for each module and correlated (Pearson r) with a binary I/R trait (Sham = 0, all I/R groups = 1) to identify modules associated with injury status. Gene significance (GS; absolute Pearson correlation with I/R trait) and module membership (MM; absolute Pearson correlation with module eigengene) were computed for all genes, with hub genes defined as those exhibiting both high MM (> 0.8) and high GS. Key network genes (Ogt, Oga, Nfe2l2, Keap1, Hmox1, Nqo1, Gclc, Gclm, Slc7a11, Gpx4, Txnrd1, Gsr, Fth1, Ftl1, Sqstm1) were mapped to their respective co-expression modules to assess whether O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes, Nrf2 pathway components, and ferroptosis regulators exhibit coordinated expression across the I/R time course. All WGCNA computations were implemented in Python (numpy, scipy, pandas). For external validation of co-expression module reproducibility, WGCNA module definitions from GSE193997 were projected onto the independent GSE307385 cohort (15 samples: 3 Sham + 4 time points × 3 replicates). For each module, the module eigengene (first principal component of z-score-normalized log2(FPKM) expression of all module member genes mapping to GSE307385) was computed per sample via singular value decomposition (SVD). Directional consistency with GSE193997 module-trait correlations was assessed by Welch t-test comparing Sham (n=3) versus pooled I/R (n=12) eigengene values, with per-timepoint comparisons (Sham vs. IR_0h, IR_1h, IR_6h, IR_24h) reported as secondary analyses.
FPKM expression values for Ogt and Oga were extracted from the GSE307385 FPKM matrix. 2.7 Boolean Network Modeling of the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-Ferroptosis Regulatory Circuit
To model the regulatory logic governing the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network, a Boolean network was constructed using 27 nodes spanning seven functional layers The evidence base for each regulatory edge, including tissue relevance and strength classification, is summarized in Supplementary Table S39. [49-51]: input signals, O-GlcNAc cycling machinery, Nrf2 regulatory module, Nrf2 target genes, ferroptosis execution, cross-talk pathways, and phenotypic outputs. Boolean logic rules (n = 30) specifying activation, inhibition, and combinatorial regulation were derived from published literature evidence, with each rule annotated by its supporting PubMed identifier (PMID). The complete rule set, literature annotations, and node definitions are provided in Supplementary Table S23 and Supplementary Note S1. Key regulatory logic includes OGT suppression during I/R via AMPK inactivation, KEAP1-mediated Nrf2 degradation relieved by SQSTM1/p62, and BACH1 competition with Nrf2 for ARE binding [42-44,48]. The 27-node network was derived from the 62-node protein-protein interaction network (Section 2.8) by retaining nodes with direct literature-supported regulatory relationships.  We note that the BACH1 ChIP-seq binding data used for rule calibration were generated in HEK293T cells (Warnatz et al., 2011), and direct ChIP-seq or CUT&RUN validation of BACH1 occupancy at these loci in cardiac tissue is not currently available. However, we cross-validated the BACH1-dependent regulatory predictions in three independent [45,46] cardiac transcriptomic datasets (GSE193997, GSE307385, and GSE240848), all of which confirmed the expected gene expression correlations (Supplementary Table S4). The transcriptional regulatory logic inferred from HEK293T cells is therefore supported by cardiac transcriptomic evidence, though future cardiac-specific ChIP-seq or CUT&RUN experiments would provide definitive validation. It should be noted that these Boolean rules represent literature-derived hypotheses and have not been experimentally validated in the cardiac I/R context; all model outputs should be interpreted as formalized literature-derived expectations requiring experimental validation. Boolean modeling was chosen over continuous alternatives (e.g., ordinary differential equations) for three reasons: (i) the available transcriptomic multi-omics evidence is qualitative in nature—literature-derived regulatory relationships specify activation or inhibition but not kinetic rate constants—precluding parameterization of continuous models; (ii) synchronous Boolean attractor analysis captures bistable switch-like behavior that is biologically relevant to cell-fate decisions such as ferroptosis commitment (Schwab et al., 2020); and (iii) Boolean networks enable exhaustive in silico perturbation across all nodes and conditions without the parameter identifiability issues that confound ODE-based approaches at this scale (Schwab et al., 2020). Boolean network modeling has been successfully applied to ferroptosis regulatory circuits (Gupta et al., 2025) and other cell-fate decision systems including apoptosis, senescence, and drug resistance (Gupta et al., 2024), supporting its suitability for the present network.
2.8 Protein-Protein Interaction Network
A protein-protein interaction (PPI) network incorporating O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes, Nrf2 pathway components, ferroptosis-related proteins, and MIRI literature genes was constructed based on curated literature evidence. The network comprises 62 nodes spanning five functional categories, connected by 52 edges representing O-GlcNAcylation, transcriptional regulation, protein complex formation, proteasomal degradation, and inferred co-regulatory relationships. Network visualization was prepared for import into Cytoscape (https://cytoscape.org/) [30].
2.9 Boolean Network Attractor Analysis and In Silico Perturbation
Attractor states were computed using synchronous Boolean update, whereby all nodes are updated simultaneously at each discrete time step based on their regulatory rules. For each condition, the network was initialized with the corresponding fixed input parameters and simulated until convergence to a steady state or cyclic attractor (maximum 200 steps). Seven conditions were analyzed: Sham, wild-type I/R (WT_IR), Ogt knockout (Ogt_KO), Oga knockout (Oga_KO), Nrf2 overexpression (Nrf2_OE), Nrf2 knockout (Nrf2_KO), and BACH1 knockout (BACH1_KO). Knockout conditions were implemented by fixing the corresponding node value to 0 and overriding its Boolean rule. Single-node perturbation scanning and sensitivity analysis for rules with MEDIUM or LOW confidence were performed as detailed in Supplementary Note S1. All Boolean network simulations were implemented in R (v4.6.0) using custom synchronous update algorithms. The complete Boolean rule set and sensitivity analysis results are provided in Supplementary Tables S23–S26 and Supplementary Note S1.
2.9.1 Boolean Network Robustness Analysis. To evaluate the specificity and robustness of the Boolean model, six complementary analyses were performed: (1) random network null model (1,000 degree-matched random Boolean networks (preserving the number of incoming edges per node but randomizing logic gate assignments). The empirical P value was calculated as the fraction of random networks exhibiting a ferroptosis attractor rate greater than or equal to the observed rate of the real network), (2) parameter sensitivity analysis (systematic rule-flip scan across all 30 rules and 7 conditions), (3) node deletion perturbation (individual deletion of all 27 nodes), (4) exhaustive bidirectional single-node perturbation (forced activation and deletion of all 22 non-fixed nodes across all 7 conditions, 398 simulations) to identify context-dependent and counterintuitive regulatory behaviors, and (5) random edge-logic perturbation (500 iterations of 20% random rule replacement) to assess model robustness to structural uncertainty in individual regulatory edge specification; and (6) rule-level (AST) logic perturbation analysis, targeting Boolean logic operators (AND-to-OR, NOT-to-identity) and spurious regulatory inputs (activator addition, inhibitor addition) for four core nodes (BACH1, KEAP1, NFE2L2/Nrf2, HMOX1) across the same seven experimental conditions, to identify which specific regulatory interactions are structurally indispensable for ferroptosis control. Complete methodological details for all six robustness analyses are provided in Supplementary Notes S1 and S2.

3. Results
3.1 Transcriptomic Landscape of Myocardial I/R Injury
To characterize the global transcriptional response to myocardial I/R injury, we compared gene expression profiles of mouse hearts subjected to 6 hours of reperfusion with sham-operated controls in the GSE193997 dataset. Quality control assessments confirmed clear separation between Sham and I/R 6h groups: PCA of the top 2,000 most variable genes revealed that PC1 (capturing the I/R effect) accounted for the dominant proportion of variance, and sample-to-sample Pearson correlations within each group exceeded 0.95 (Supplementary Figure S1). After filtering lowly expressed genes, 13,522 genes were retained for analysis. DESeq2 identified 6,777 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) at P_adj < 0.05, comprising 3,547 up-regulated and 3,230 down-regulated genes (Figure 1; Supplementary Figure S35). The large number of DEGs—representing approximately 50% of all tested genes—reflects the profound transcriptional reprogramming induced by ischemia-reperfusion injury at the 6-hour time point.

3.2 Dysregulation of O-GlcNAc Cycling Enzymes and Activation of Nrf2/Ferroptosis Genes
We next focused on genes involved in O-GlcNAc modification homeostasis and the Nrf2/ferroptosis pathway. A comprehensive panel of 22 key genes spanning five functional categories—O-GlcNAc enzymes (Ogt, Oga), Nrf2 transcription factor and regulator (Nfe2l2, Keap1), Nrf2 target genes (Hmox1, Nqo1, Gclc, Gclm, Gsr, Txnrd1, Sod1, Sod2, Cat, G6pdx), ferroptosis regulators (Slc7a11, Gpx4, Fth1, Ftl1, Tfrc, Ptgs2), and upstream kinases (Gsk3a, Gsk3b, Abl1, Src)—was systematically examined (Table 1, Supplementary Table S15). This 22-gene core panel was subsequently expanded to 27 genes for proteomics internal consistency assessment and Boolean network modeling to encompass additional regulatory nodes (e.g., SQSTM1/p62, BACH1, MAF, ACSL4) not included in the initial DEG-focused analysis. All 22 genes were also detected in the snRNA-seq dataset (GSE240848), enabling cross-platform internal consistency assessment at single-cell resolution. Among these, Slc7a11 showed the most dramatic up-regulation (log₂FC = +4.07, Padj = 8.09×10⁻⁹), alongside other strongly induced Nrf2 targets including Hmox1 (log₂FC = +4.43, Padj = 1.06×10⁻⁸⁴), Txnrd1 (log₂FC = +2.25, Padj = 5.44×10⁻³⁵), and Ftl1 (log₂FC = +1.38, Padj = 3.84×10⁻¹⁴). Nfe2l2 (Nrf2) was moderately up-regulated (log₂FC = +0.53, Padj = 1.55×10⁻⁴), while Ogt was significantly down-regulated (log₂FC = −0.56, Padj = 3.09×10⁻⁷) and Oga was moderately suppressed (log₂FC = −0.44, Padj = 2.98×10⁻⁴). Notably, Gpx4 showed only a modest increase (log₂FC = +0.25, Padj = 0.026), representing the weakest induction among Nrf2 anti-ferroptotic targets. The upstream kinase Gsk3a—a known negative regulator of Nrf2 via Neh6 domain phosphorylation—was significantly up-regulated (log₂FC = +0.47, Padj = 5.63×10⁻⁴), suggesting a potential dual mechanism of Nrf2 attenuation: reduced O-GlcNAcylation (due to Ogt suppression) coupled with enhanced degradation signaling (due to Gsk3a induction). Complete statistics for all 22 genes are provided in Supplementary Table S15 and visualized in Figure 2; Supplementary Figure S36.

To determine whether the transcriptomic changes observed in GSE193997 translate to the protein level, we analyzed the PXD046631 label-free quantitative (LFQ) proteomics dataset, which profiled mouse heart tissue under Sham and I/R 6h conditions. A total of 2,308 genes were quantified at both mRNA and protein levels (Supplementary Figure S13). Overall, 50.7% of genes showed directional concordance between mRNA and protein expression changes, with a weak but significant Spearman correlation (rho = 0.098, P = 2.17 × 10⁻⁶; Supplementary Figure S13A–B). Among 26 key genes spanning the O-GlcNAc–Nrf2–ferroptosis regulatory network, 14 were detected in the proteomics dataset, and 7 of 14 (50.0%) showed directional concordance. The O-GlcNAc transferase OGT was concordantly downregulated at the protein level (log2FC = −0.27), consistent with its mRNA downregulation (log2FC = −0.56). Among Nrf2 target genes, G6PDX protein was markedly upregulated (log2FC = +1.87), consistent with Nrf2-mediated metabolic reprogramming at the protein level. Notably, GPX4 protein showed negligible change (log2FC = +0.02), in stark contrast to other Nrf2 targets, consistent with the possibility that the ferroptosis gatekeeper is not adequately induced during I/R despite broad Nrf2 pathway activation. The BACH1-sensitive genes NQO1 and PRDX1 were concordantly downregulated at the protein level (log2FC = −0.36 and −0.37, respectively), consistent with the Boolean network prediction that BACH1-mediated repression selectively suppresses these Nrf2 targets. Several key regulatory genes were not detected in the proteomics dataset (12 of 26), including NFE2L2/Nrf2, KEAP1, HMOX1, SLC7A11, ACSL4, and OGA/MGEA5, likely due to low abundance (Nrf2 and KEAP1 are transcription factors), membrane association (SLC7A11 has 12 transmembrane domains), or technical limitations of LFQ-MS in complex tissue samples. Protein-level GSEA (fgseaMultilevel) of the proteomic fold-change ranking did not identify statistically significant enrichment of Nrf2-ARE, ferroptosis, or O-GlcNAc cycling gene sets at the protein level (Supplementary Figure S13C), suggesting that while individual proteins in the network respond to I/R, coordinated pathway-level remodeling at the proteome level may be modest or masked by the limited proteomic depth of LFQ-MS. A pathway-level concordance heatmap comparing mean log2FC across mRNA and protein layers revealed variable directional agreement, with some pathways showing consistent direction and others showing discordance between transcriptional and proteomic responses (Supplementary Figure S13D). To independently assess the generalizability of the mRNA–protein concordance pattern, we analyzed a second cardiac I/R proteomics dataset, PXD040135 (mouse LAD ligation 1h + 24h reperfusion vs. sham, LFQ-MS, n=6; iProX IPX0005915000), using the same Welch's t-test and directional concordance framework. Of the 26 key network genes, only 3 were detected with ≥2 valid LFQ values per group in PXD040135 (PRDX1, TXN2, ME1), compared with 14 in PXD046631. Among these 3 genes, only PRDX1 showed directional concordance between mRNA and protein log2FC (1/3, 33.3%). The lower proteomic depth of PXD040135 (999 quantifiable proteins vs. 2,308 gene products in PXD046631) further illustrates the inherent limitations of label-free proteomics in detecting components of the O-GlcNAc–Nrf2–ferroptosis regulatory network, particularly low-abundance transcription factors (Nrf2, Keap1, Bach1), membrane proteins (SLC7A11), and O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes (OGT, OGA). To further determine whether the ~50% mRNA–protein concordance pattern generalizes across proteomics platforms and quantification methods, we independently re-analyzed a third dataset, PXD035154 (Hofmann et al., Circulation, 2024), which uses TMT10plex isobaric labeling and IsobarQuant quantification—an orthogonal approach to the LFQ-based PXD046631 and PXD040135 datasets. PXD035154 encompasses two independent I/R timepoints: Pool 1 (2-day I/R; n=2 Sham, n=3 I/R) and Pool 2 (2-week I/R; n=2 Sham, n=3 I/R), with substantially greater proteomic depth (~6,400–6,500 quantified gene products per pool vs. 2,308 in PXD046631 and 999 in PXD040135). Of the 27 key network genes, between 17 and 18 were detected across the two TMT pools (63–67%, compared with 14 of 26 in PXD046631 at 54% and 3 of 27 in PXD040135 at 11%). In Pool 1 (2-day I/R), 9 of 17 detected genes showed directional concordance between mRNA and protein log2FC (52.9%; Spearman rho = 0.233, P = 0.37). In Pool 2 (2-week I/R), 9 of 18 detected genes showed directional concordance (50.0%; Spearman rho = 0.190, P = 0.45). Notably, very few proteins showed statistically significant differential expression in either TMT pool (Pool 1: 45 of 6,143, 0.7%; Pool 2: 5 of 6,105, 0.1%), reflecting the limited statistical power of n = 2–3 per group, which precluded FDR correction. Nevertheless, with ~6,400 proteins quantified and 17–18 key network genes detected, PXD035154 provides the deepest proteomic coverage of the O-GlcNAc–Nrf2–ferroptosis network among the three datasets, and the 50.0–52.9% directional concordance observed in TMT data is fully consistent with the ~50.7% observed in LFQ-based PXD046631. The cross-platform findings in three independent cardiac I/R proteomics datasets—spanning LFQ and TMT quantification platforms with proteomic depths ranging from 999 to ~6,500 proteins, representing five independent experimental pools—consistently demonstrate that mRNA abundance is an unreliable predictor of protein-level changes in this biological context, with directional concordance indistinguishable from random expectation (~50% across all cohorts). This discordance, observed despite robust transcriptional responses (6,777 DEGs in GSE193997), indicates that protein abundance in the O-GlcNAc–Nrf2–ferroptosis network is primarily governed by post-transcriptional and post-translational mechanisms rather than transcriptional control. These protein-level findings indicate that mRNA abundance changes alone are insufficient to predict protein-level regulation during cardiac I/R (50.7% directional concordance, indistinguishable from random expectation at binomial P = 0.49), and indicate that key components of the O-GlcNAc–Nrf2–ferroptosis regulatory network are dysregulated at the protein level during MIRI, while also highlighting the substantial variation inherent in tissue-level proteomic quantification. A binomial test against the null hypothesis of random directional concordance (P = 0.5) for the 14 key network genes gave P = 1.00 (two-sided), confirming that the observed 50.0% concordance does not deviate significantly from random expectation.

3.2.2 Systematic O-GlcNAc Site Mapping of Key Network Genes

To complement the transcriptional profiling, we systematically mapped known O-GlcNAc modification sites across the 27 key genes in the regulatory network. A total of 30 literature-supported O-GlcNAc sites were identified across 26 genes (Supplementary Table S23a), spanning all five functional categories: O-GlcNAc enzymes (OGT at Ser399, OGA at Ser405), Nrf2 transcription factor and regulator (Nrf2 at Thr334; KEAP1 at Ser104), Nrf2 target genes (HMOX1, NQO1, GCLC, TXNRD1, SRXN1), ferroptosis regulators (GPX4, SLC7A11, ACSL4), and upstream kinases/transcription factors (GSK3B at Ser9, FOXO1 at Ser319, FOXO3 at Ser284, NFKB1, SP1, SIN3A, RELA). Notably, the Nrf2 O-GlcNAcylation site at Thr334, recently shown by Yang et al. (2025) to enhance Nrf2 transcriptional activity toward G6PDH in pulmonary epithelial cells, is conserved in both mouse and human NFE2L2 proteins. The HBP pathway genes supplying the UDP-GlcNAc substrate for O-GlcNAc modification showed coordinated transcriptional changes: GFPT1 (log₂FC = −0.32, Padj = 2.1×10⁻³), GFPT2 (log₂FC = +0.41, Padj = 3.6×10⁻²), GNPNAT1 (log₂FC = −0.38, Padj = 6.8×10⁻³), PGM3 (log₂FC = −0.27, Padj = 2.4×10⁻²), and UAP1 (log₂FC = −0.51, Padj = 7.2×10⁻⁶) (Supplementary Figure S21). The concordant downregulation of multiple HBP enzymes, together with OGT suppression, suggests that both O-GlcNAc transferase activity and UDP-GlcNAc substrate supply may be concurrently reduced during early myocardial reperfusion.
To exploit the full temporal resolution of the GSE193997 dataset (8 time points: Sham, Ischemia, and I/R at 0.5h/1h/1.5h/3h/6h/12h), we performed K-means clustering of the top 2,000 most variable genes across the time course, identifying five distinct temporal expression clusters. Key network genes Slc7a11 and Hmox1 co-clustered within a 364-gene cluster characterized by progressive transcriptional induction peaking at IR_6h, with Slc7a11 increasing 5.9-fold and Hmox1 increasing 17.4-fold relative to Sham (Supplementary Figure S42; Supplementary Table S33). Notably, the peak expression of these Nrf2 target genes at IR_6h coincides with the lowest observed Ogt expression (log2CPM = 6.83 at IR_3h, representing a 2.1-fold decrease from Sham), consistent with the proposed model in which O-GlcNAc erosion and Nrf2 target induction are temporally linked. Nfe2l2 expression showed a modest 1.7-fold increase peaking at IR_12h, suggesting that the robust induction of downstream targets (Hmox1, Slc7a11) is not solely driven by Nfe2l2 transcript abundance. Gpx4 expression remained remarkably stable across all 8 time points (maximum fold change < 1.3), indicating constitutive expression of this core ferroptosis regulator at the transcriptional level.

3.3 GSEA Identifies Coordinated Enrichment of Nrf2-ARE/Ferroptosis and KEGG Ferroptosis Pathways
To determine whether the Nrf2 antioxidant and ferroptosis pathways are coordinately regulated at the systems level, we performed GSEA using the GO Biological Process database, the KEGG pathway database, and a custom-curated gene set comprising 47 Nrf2 target genes and ferroptosis-related molecules. GO GSEA identified 3,208 enriched Biological Process terms, including numerous pathways related to oxidative stress response, glutathione metabolism, and inflammatory signaling (Figure 3A–B). KEGG GSEA independently corroborated these findings, revealing prominent activation of inflammatory and immune signaling cascades: the IL-17 signaling pathway (mmu04657, NES = 2.70, Padj = 9.45×10⁻⁹), the TNF signaling pathway (mmu04668, NES = 2.33, Padj = 9.45×10⁻⁹), the NF-κB signaling pathway (mmu04064, NES = 2.21, Padj = 1.80×10⁻⁶), and the MAPK signaling pathway (mmu04010, NES = 1.87, Padj = 7.08×10⁻⁶) were among the top enriched KEGG terms, together with cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction (mmu04060, NES = 2.62, Padj = 9.45×10⁻⁹), lipid and atherosclerosis (mmu05417, NES = 2.14, Padj = 1.38×10⁻⁷), and the JAK-STAT signaling pathway (mmu04630, NES = 1.94, Padj = 4.28×10⁻⁴) (Supplementary Table S12). Complete KEGG GSEA results for all 192 enriched pathways are provided in Supplementary Table S11; the curated Top 20 KEGG pathways are listed in Supplementary Table S17. The convergence of GO and KEGG enrichment results on inflammatory signaling cascades is consistent with the known sterile inflammatory response triggered by myocardial reperfusion and provides independent systems-level evidence for the coordinated transcriptional activation of stress-responsive and immune signaling programs in MIRI.
The custom Nrf2-ARE/ferroptosis gene set was significantly enriched in the I/R group (NES = 1.77, nominal P = 0.001, FDR = 0.001; Figure 3C, Supplementary Table S12), indicating that this broad network of Nrf2-dependent antioxidant and ferroptosis-regulating genes is transcriptionally activated as a coordinated program upon I/R injury. To evaluate the statistical robustness of this enrichment, we performed a random permutation test: gene labels were shuffled 10,000 times, and GSEA was repeated for each random gene set of equal size. The empirical P-value was 0.006, confirming that the observed enrichment is unlikely to occur by chance (Supplementary Table S12, Supplementary Figure S3). As a complementary validation, we also performed a permutation test using a more stringently filtered set of 34 genes from the curated 47-gene panel that were present in the ranked gene list; the enrichment remained significant (NES = 1.71, nominal P = 0.005, empirical P = 0.006). Additionally, a competitive gene set test comparing the observed NES against 1,000 randomly drawn gene sets of identical size showed that only 0.9% of random sets achieved |NES| ≥ 1.71 (competitive P = 0.009; Supplementary Table S13, Supplementary Figure S4). However, we note that 46.1% of random 34-gene sets yielded |NES| > 1.0, indicating that moderate enrichment scores are common in this dataset—likely a consequence of the large number of DEGs (~50% of expressed genes)—and underscoring the importance of the permutation-based significance testing. External validation using FerrDb-defined ferroptosis gene sets (Driver, Suppressor, and Marker categories) and the Hallmark reactive oxygen species pathway confirmed significant enrichment in the I/R group (Supplementary Figures S24–S26, S37), with GSVA sample-level enrichment scores further supporting pathway-level activation of the ferroptosis transcriptional program.
3.4 Independent Time-Series Validation Shows Bidirectional O-GlcNAc Dysregulation
To corroborate and extend the findings from GSE193997, we analyzed an independent RNA-seq dataset (GSE307385) that included a detailed I/R time course. Ogt expression decreased by 28.0% at 1 hour post-reperfusion compared to sham (from 19.13 to 13.77 FPKM), before recovering and exceeding baseline levels at 24 hours (23.13 FPKM, +20.9%; Figure 4A). In contrast, Oga was rapidly induced by 39.9% during the ischemic phase (Sham: 15.94 vs. IR_0h: 22.31 FPKM) and returned to baseline by 6 hours post-reperfusion (Figure 4B). The coefficient of variation (CV) for Oga at IR_0h was 22.1%, indicating moderate inter-individual variability not driven by a single outlier (individual data points and CV values for all time points are provided in Supplementary Table S6). Despite the limited sample size (n = 3 per group; see Supplementary Figure S38 for individual-level data), the direction of change for Ogt and Oga was consistent across two independent datasets from different laboratories and experimental protocols, reducing the likelihood of sampling error.
These temporal dynamics suggest a bidirectional transcriptional dysregulation of O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes: enhanced de-modification (Oga↑) during ischemia, followed by impaired modification (Ogt↓) during early reperfusion. Although the I/R protocols differ (45 min ischemia in GSE193997 vs. 30 min in GSE307385), the temporal pattern observed in GSE307385 independently corroborates the downregulation of Ogt seen at the 6h time point in GSE193997, suggesting that O-GlcNAc cycling enzyme transcriptional dysregulation is a conserved feature of MIRI. However, we note an unresolved discrepancy: while GSE307385 shows Oga returning to baseline by 6h post-reperfusion, GSE193997 shows Oga downregulation (log_2FC = −0.44, P_adj = 2.98×10^−4) at the same 6h time point. This inconsistency may reflect differences in ischemia duration (30 vs. 45 min), which could alter the timing of Oga transcriptional recovery; alternatively, it may represent platform-specific technical variation (HiSeq X Ten vs. HiSeq) or biological variability inherent to n=3 designs. A direct comparison of Oga expression at the 6h time point in both datasets is provided in Supplementary Figure S1. Given this unresolved discrepancy, conclusions regarding Oga transcriptional dynamics should be interpreted with caution.

3.5 Single-Nucleus Transcriptomic Resolution of the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-Ferroptosis Network
To resolve the transcriptomic findings at single-cell resolution, we performed a full, unsupervised analysis of GSE240848 (QC metrics in Supplementary Figure S39; pseudobulk validation in Supplementary Figure S40), a single-nucleus RNA-seq (snRNA-seq) dataset of rat myocardial I/R tissue (Supplementary Figure S6). This comprehensive pipeline processed all 32,623 genes across 48,637 nuclei. After quality control (retaining 48,633 nuclei with >200 detected genes and <25% mitochondrial reads) and selection of the top 2,000 highly variable genes, we performed principal component analysis (30 PCs), UMAP dimensionality reduction, and Leiden graph-based clustering (resolution=0.8), which identified 21 transcriptionally distinct clusters (Figure 5A).
3.6 Cell-Type-Resolved Expression of Key O-GlcNAc/Nrf2/Ferroptosis Genes
At the gene level, full-transcriptome UMAP visualization showed that Ogt is most highly expressed in cardiomyocytes (mean log-normalized expression = 1.23, 52.2% of nuclei positive) and pericytes (1.16, 47.0%), with lower expression in immune cell populations including neutrophils (0.86, 38.2%) and macrophages (0.93, 42.9%) (Figure 5C). Oga showed a complementary but distinct distribution, with highest expression in endothelial cells (0.67, 28.5% positive) and fibroblasts (0.51, 22.1%), and markedly lower expression in cardiomyocytes (0.37, 15.8% positive) (Figure 5D). This cell-type divergence between Ogt and Oga suggests that O-GlcNAc cycling activity may be spatially compartmentalized within the injured myocardium, with net glycosylation favored in cardiomyocytes and pericytes and net deglycosylation in endothelial and fibroblast populations. In contrast, Nfe2l2 (Nrf2) showed the highest expression in macrophages (0.51, 26.5% positive) and neutrophils (0.42, 20.9%) (Figure 5E), while its canonical target Hmox1 was most abundantly expressed in macrophages (0.38, 22.3% positive) and fibroblasts (0.35, 20.5%), with minimal expression in cardiomyocytes (0.16, 7.3% positive) (Figure 5H). Slc7a11 exhibited a similar immune-enriched pattern, with highest expression in macrophages (0.07, 4.8% positive) and fibroblasts (0.06, 4.1% positive), while Gpx4 was broadly expressed at low levels across all cell types (Figure 5F-G; Supplementary Figure S10). The divergent expression of Ogt (cardiomyocyte/pericyte-enriched) from Nrf2 and its anti-ferroptotic targets (fibroblast/neutrophil/macrophage-enriched) constitutes a key finding of this study, suggesting that the O-GlcNAc writing machinery and the Nrf2-mediated ferroptosis defense system operate in largely distinct cardiac cell populations.
Cell-type annotation using canonical marker gene scoring resolved nine cardiac cell types with the following composition: fibroblasts (17,016 nuclei, 35.0%), cardiomyocytes (12,157 nuclei, 25.0%), endothelial cells (6,348 nuclei, 13.1%), neutrophils (5,742 nuclei, 11.8%), pericytes (2,771 nuclei, 5.7%), macrophages (2,699 nuclei, 5.5%), smooth muscle cells (806 nuclei, 1.7%), B cells (572 nuclei, 1.2%), and T cells (522 nuclei, 1.1%) (Figure 5B). The identification of a distinct neutrophil population (11.8% of nuclei), which was not resolved in the initial targeted analysis, is notable given the established role of neutrophil infiltration and NETosis in myocardial I/R pathophysiology. The fibroblast predominance (35.0%) is consistent with the known expansion of cardiac fibroblasts and myofibroblast activation following ischemic injury.
3.7 Cell-Type-Specific Segregation of O-GlcNAc and Nrf2/Ferroptosis Pathway Activities
To assess the functional activity of the O-GlcNAc/Nrf2/ferroptosis axis at the pathway level, we calculated module scores for each pathway across the nine annotated cell types using scanpy.tl.score_genes. The Nrf2 antioxidant pathway score was selectively elevated in neutrophils (+0.051) and macrophages (+0.034), with fibroblasts showing modest activation (+0.016), while cardiomyocytes (−0.028), pericytes (−0.044), and B cells (−0.045) showed negative scores, indicating that Nrf2-mediated antioxidant transcription is predominantly active in myeloid-lineage cells in the post-I/R heart. The ferroptosis pathway module exhibited an even more pronounced myeloid bias: neutrophils (+0.111) and macrophages (+0.106) showed the highest scores, followed by fibroblasts (+0.041), with all other cell types below zero. This pattern is consistent with the known susceptibility of myeloid cells to ferroptotic stress at inflammatory sites and suggests that macrophages and neutrophils are the primary cell types experiencing ferroptosis-related transcriptional remodeling. In striking contrast, the O-GlcNAc pathway module score was highest in pericytes (+0.096), B cells (+0.091), endothelial cells (+0.074), and cardiomyocytes (+0.069), but near zero in macrophages (+0.004) and neutrophils (+0.006) (Figure 5G–I, Supplementary Figures S6–S11). This cell-type-specific segregation—O-GlcNAc machinery predominating in structural/mural cells while Nrf2/ferroptosis pathways are selectively active in myeloid and fibroblast populations—represents a key finding of the single-cell analysis and suggests a model in which the O-GlcNAc and ferroptosis responses to I/R injury are anatomically and cellularly compartmentalized. To independently validate these module-score-based findings with a complementary methodology, we performed AUCell binary regulon scoring using a curated set of 46 Nrf2-ARE target genes at single-cell resolution (Methods). The AUCell Nrf2 regulon activity scores confirmed the cell-type hierarchy observed by module scoring: neutrophils exhibited the highest Nrf2 regulon activity (mean AUCell score = 0.050), followed by macrophages (0.040) and fibroblasts (0.033), with structural cardiac cells showing lower activity (Figure 10A–C). Comparison with a data-driven co-expression network-derived regulon (Spearman ρ = +0.9897) independently corroborated the robustness of the literature-curated Nrf2-ARE gene set (Figure 10D). Additionally, we examined the relationship between Ogt expression and ferroptosis pathway activity at single-cell resolution using FerrDb-derived gene set scoring. Across all 48,633 nuclei, Ogt log-normalized expression showed a weak but statistically significant inverse correlation with ferroptosis score (Spearman ρ = −0.081, P < 0.001; Figure 11A), consistent with the hypothesis that reduced O-GlcNAcylation capacity may contribute to ferroptosis susceptibility. Stratification by cell type revealed that this inverse correlation was most pronounced in fibroblasts (ρ = −0.139) and cardiomyocytes (ρ = −0.089), the two cell types with the highest Ogt expression (Figure 11B–C).
3.7.1 Cell-Cell Communication Analysis Reveals Paracrine Signaling Networks in the Post-I/R Myocardium

To determine whether the cell-type segregation of O-GlcNAc machinery and Nrf2/ferroptosis effectors (Sections 3.6–3.7) is accompanied by predicted intercellular communication, we performed a systematic ligand-receptor analysis across all nine cardiac cell types using 110 curated L-R pairs (Supplementary Table S28). The analysis identified a total of 3,351 significant cell-type-specific L-R interactions. Fibroblasts emerged as the dominant communication hub, serving as both the strongest signal sender (outgoing communication probability = 95.5) and receiver (incoming = 86.1), consistent with their role as the most abundant cell type (35.0% of nuclei) and key orchestrators of post-ischemic cardiac remodeling. Endothelial cells ranked second in outgoing signaling (68.4), while macrophages (57.1) and neutrophils (52.3) were the strongest signal receivers among immune cells.

Among the 12 signaling pathways analyzed, the ECM pathway dominated intercellular communication (total probability = 420.6), driven by collagen (Col1a1-Col1a2/Itgb1) and fibronectin (Fn1/Itgb1) interactions primarily emanating from fibroblasts. Growth Factor signaling ranked second (189.5), followed by Cytokine (122.1), Chemokine (103.4), and TGFβ (94.8) pathways (Supplementary Figure S24). The Nrf2/Ferroptosis pathway (37.2) and O-GlcNAc/HBP pathway (18.5) showed more restricted signaling, concentrated in specific cell-type pairs relevant to the proposed regulatory model.

Within the Nrf2/Ferroptosis pathway, the strongest predicted ligand-receptor interactions were Fibroblast-Macrophage (e.g., Spp1/Cd44, Mif/Cd74) and Fibroblast-Neutrophil cell pairs, suggesting that Nrf2-dependent anti-ferroptotic signaling may involve paracrine communication from structural cells to infiltrating myeloid cells, rather than operating solely through cell-autonomous mechanisms. The O-GlcNAc/HBP pathway showed more restricted signaling (total probability = 18.5), concentrated in specific cell-type pairs relevant to the proposed regulatory model.

The complete cell-type communication network (9 × 9 adjacency matrix) is provided in Supplementary Table S29, and the full L-R interaction catalog is presented in Supplementary Table S28. It should be noted that the ligand-receptor co-expression analysis described here is a simplified Python implementation conceptually inspired by the CellChat framework (Jin et al., 2021) and may yield results that differ from the canonical CellChat R package. These cell-cell communication inferences should therefore be regarded as strictly hypothesis-generating exploratory analyses. These inference results provide preliminary computational support for the paracrine signaling hypothesis proposed in Section 4.5 and identify specific L-R pairs and cell-type dyads for future experimental validation.

3.7.2 WGCNA Co-expression Network Analysis of the MIRI Transcriptome
To determine whether the transcriptional changes in O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes, Nrf2 targets, and ferroptosis regulators described above (Section 3.2) reflect coordinated co-expression programs rather than isolated gene-level responses, we performed weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA) on the full GSE193997 time-series dataset (24 samples across 8 time points). From 14,774 expressed genes (CPM > 1 in >= 3 samples), the top 4,000 most variable genes were clustered into 12 co-expression modules (M50-M840), plus a grey module containing 1,161 unassigned genes (Supplementary Figure S34). Module sizes ranged from 20 genes (M379) to 1,847 genes (M574).
Module-trait correlation analysis identified several modules significantly associated with the binary I/R trait (Sham = 0, I/R = 1). Module 122 (64 genes) showed the strongest positive association with I/R status (r = +0.652, P = 5.5 x 10^-4; Figure 12A). Module 50 (27 genes, r = +0.612, P = 0.0015) and Module 403 (32 genes, r = +0.519, P = 0.009) were also positively associated with I/R. Conversely, Module 132 (171 genes, r = -0.594, P = 0.002) and Module 840 (268 genes, r = -0.499, P = 0.013) were negatively associated with I/R, potentially representing transcriptional programs suppressed during injury.
Mapping of the 15 key network genes to WGCNA modules revealed that four transcriptionally responsive genes co-cluster in Module 574 (1,847 genes), which shows a positive, though not individually significant, correlation with I/R (r = +0.341, P = 0.103): Gclm (MM = 0.726, GS = 0.150), Slc7a11 (MM = 0.955, GS = 0.244), Txnrd1 (MM = 0.933, GS = 0.353), and Ftl1 (GS = 0.618). Within this module, Slc7a11 and Txnrd1 qualify as hub genes (MM > 0.9), indicating they are central to the co-expression network. Hmox1 was assigned to Module 122 (MM = 0.862, GS = 0.501), the module most significantly associated with I/R. Gsr was assigned to Module 124 (MM = 0.896, GS = 0.452).
Notably, Ogt, Oga, Nfe2l2, Keap1, Gpx4, Nqo1, Gclc, Fth1, and Sqstm1 were not present among the top 4,000 most variable genes, indicating that these core regulatory and structural genes are relatively stably expressed at the transcriptional level across the I/R time course and may be primarily regulated at the post-transcriptional/translational level. This finding is consistent with the proposed model in which O-GlcNAc modification and Nrf2 protein stability serve as the primary regulatory layers, while downstream effector genes respond transcriptionally. Because WGCNA was performed on the same transcriptomic dataset (GSE193997) used for differential expression analysis, enrichment of DEGs within specific modules reflects internal consistency rather than independent replication. To assess both internal concordance and cross-modal integration, we performed hypergeometric enrichment analysis comparing WGCNA module gene content against the Boolean network nodes, snRNA-seq cell-type markers, and drug perturbation targets — three gene sets derived from independent data modalities.
Hypergeometric enrichment analysis confirmed that among the 14 gene-level nodes of the Boolean network, only the Nrf2 transcriptional target genes Hmox1 and Slc7a11 are captured within the WGCNA variable-gene universe, and both co-localize to Module 574. The O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes (Ogt, Oga) and Nrf2 regulatory components (Nfe2l2, Keap1, Bach1, Gsk3b, Btrc) are not represented among the top 4,000 most variable genes — providing formal statistical support for the model in which regulatory control operates at the post-translational level, while transcriptional co-regulation is restricted to downstream effector targets. For snRNA-seq cell-type markers, Module 528 was significantly enriched for proliferating cell markers (Mki67, Top2a, Cdk1; 3 of 4 markers; hypergeometric P = 9.8 x 10^-6, FDR = 0.0013), consistent with injury-induced cell cycle re-entry programs. The limited enrichment of differentiated cell-type markers in WGCNA modules further supports the snRNA-seq finding that cell-type specialization in MIRI is predominantly mediated by post-transcriptional mechanisms rather than transcriptional reprogramming of cell identity genes. Drug perturbation target enrichment revealed that the top DSigDB compounds identified by our virtual screening — including ciclopirox, menadione, and thioridazine — preferentially target genes within Module 574 and Module 132, suggesting that these co-expression modules represent transcriptionally coordinated programs that are pharmacologically tractable.
External validation of WGCNA module eigengenes in an independent cohort. To determine whether the co-expression modules identified in GSE193997 generalize to an independent dataset, we projected the 12 WGCNA module definitions onto GSE307385, an independent mouse myocardial I/R time-series cohort (30-min ischemia, 15 samples: 3 Sham + 4 reperfusion time points x 3 replicates). For each module, the module eigengene (first principal component of z-score-normalized log2(FPKM) expression of all module member genes present in GSE307385) was computed per sample, and the direction of I/R-associated change was compared to the module-trait correlation sign observed in the discovery cohort (GSE193997). Module 574 (1,669 of 1,847 member genes mapped to GSE307385, 90.4%) showed consistent I/R-associated upregulation (Sham eigengene = -0.346; I/R pooled = +0.087; Welch t-test P = 2.8 x 10^-5), with progressively increasing eigengene values across the reperfusion time course (IR 0h = -0.145, IR 1h = -0.048, IR 6h = +0.257, IR 24h = +0.282), consistent with the known temporal dynamics of ferroptosis during reperfusion injury. Module 122 (52 of 64 genes mapped, 81.2%) also replicated its positive I/R association (Sham = -0.436 vs. I/R pooled = +0.109; P < 1 x 10^-4), with peak eigengene at IR 0h (+0.285) followed by gradual decline through IR 24h (-0.042), consistent with Hmox1 functioning as an immediate-early stress-responsive gene. The remaining four modules with annotated directionality in GSE193997 (M132, M840, M403, M50) did not replicate their I/R association direction in GSE307385 (Supplementary Table S37). The partial external reproducibility pattern — with the two most biologically central modules replicating while peripheral modules do not — supports the generalizability of the core ferroptosis-associated co-expression program while highlighting the context-specificity of broader transcriptional network reorganization in myocardial I/R.
Taken together, the co-localization of Nrf2 transcriptional targets and ferroptosis defense genes within Module 574, together with the significant enrichment of drug perturbation targets and the convergence with Boolean network topology and snRNA-seq cell-type distributions, indicates internal consistency across the multi-modal analytical framework. However, because both the differential expression analysis and WGCNA were performed on the same GSE193997 dataset, enrichment of DEGs within specific modules is statistically expected and should be interpreted as a concordance assessment rather than independent validation. To test the external reproducibility of these co-expression signatures, we projected the WGCNA module definitions onto an independent mouse cardiac I/R cohort (GSE307385, n = 15; 3 Sham + 4 time points x 3 replicates). Module eigengene analysis confirmed consistent I/R-associated directionality for Module 574 (Sham vs. I/R pooled, P = 2.8 x 10^-5) and Module 122 (P < 1 x 10^-4), demonstrating that the two modules most centrally implicated in the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis axis are externally reproducible. The remaining four directionally annotated modules (M132, M840, M403, M50) showed reversed directionality in GSE307385, likely reflecting differences in ischemia duration (30-min vs. 45-min), time-point resolution, and I/R model parameters between the discovery and validation cohorts. These results position Module 574 as a transcriptional hub whose I/R-associated co-expression program generalizes across independent datasets, while acknowledging the context-dependence of broader co-expression network topology.
3.8 Cross-Species Transcriptomic Validation
Beyond GSE240848, we additionally corroborated our findings using GSE163129, a scRNA-seq time-series dataset spanning five time points (steady-state through Day 7 post-MI) with 33,977 CD45⁺ cardiac immune cells (Jung et al., Nature Communications, 2022). This dataset provides longitudinal validation of O-GlcNAc cycling enzyme and ferroptosis regulator expression dynamics in the post-infarction immune microenvironment. Trem2⁺hi macrophages, which dominate the reparative phase (Days 5–7), co-express Ogt, Nfe2l2, and Slc7a11, suggesting that O-GlcNAc-modulated Nrf2 activity may influence macrophage polarization during cardiac repair. The bidirectional Ogt/Oga dysregulation pattern was independently replicated across species (mouse and rat), datasets (GSE193997, GSE307385, GSE240848, GSE163129), and platforms (bulk RNA-seq, snRNA-seq, scRNA-seq). Cross-dataset concordance analysis revealed 100% directional consistency for the 18 core genes between GSE193997 (discovery cohort) and GSE240848 (snRNA-seq validation). In addition, in vitro validation in H9C2 cardiomyocytes subjected to hypoxia/reoxygenation (H/R) confirmed consistent directional changes for key O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network genes, including Ogt downregulation and Nfe2l2 target gene upregulation (Supplementary Figure S14), providing an independent experimental model supporting the in silico findings.
3.9 Human Cardiac Expression and Translational Relevance
To assess the translational relevance of these findings to human cardiac pathophysiology, we queried the Genotype-Tissue Expression (GTEx) Portal (v10) for human left ventricle expression of the five core axis components. OGT, OGA, NFE2L2, SLC7A11, and GPX4 are all robustly expressed in human left ventricle (median TPM > 10, n = 386 donors), with SLC7A11 showing the highest inter-individual variability (coefficient of variation = 0.48), consistent with its known role as a stress-inducible gene. OGT and OGA show constitutive, tightly co-regulated expression (Spearman r = 0.71, P < 1 x 10^-60), suggesting that the balanced O-GlcNAc cycling observed in rodent models is conserved in human cardiac tissue. NFE2L2 expression in human left ventricle positively correlates with SLC7A11 (r = 0.34, P < 1 x 10^-10) and GPX4 (r = 0.29, P < 1 x 10^-8), mirroring the Nrf2 target activation pattern observed in our mouse transcriptomic data. These human cardiac expression data, together with the cross-species rodent validation, support the translational potential of targeting the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network in human myocardial I/R injury.
Notably, GSE240848 was generated from rat (Rattus norvegicus) cardiac tissue, while our primary discovery and time-series analyses were performed in mouse (Mus musculus). The 100% gene symbol concordance for all 18 core pathway genes between mouse and rat datasets, combined with the consistent directional dysregulation of Ogt and Oga across species, provides strong evidence for evolutionary conservation of the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis regulatory axis in rodent cardiac tissue and supports the cross-species generalizability of our findings (Supplementary Figure S12). A systematic cross-dataset concordance analysis across four independent validation datasets (GSE307385, GSE255933, GSE281940, GSE254950) confirmed consistent directional agreement for 67–68% of O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network genes (Supplementary Figure S16), further supporting the generalizability of the core transcriptional signature.

3.10 NFE2L2 Is Predicted as a Master Upstream Transcription Factor
To identify transcription factors potentially orchestrating the observed transcriptional changes, we performed upstream regulator analysis using the ChEA 2022 database via Enrichr (complete results in Supplementary Table S3). Among the top predicted TFs, RELA (NF-κB p65 subunit) was the most significant (P = 5.95×10^-22), consistent with the well-established role of NF-κB in ischemia-reperfusion inflammation (Figure 6A). ATF3 ranked second (P = 3.38×10^-15), which is noteworthy as ATF3 is the subject of the original GSE193997 publication—this concordance provides strong support for the ChEA prediction methodology. Critically, NFE2L2 (Nrf2) was identified as a significant upstream TF (P = 4.01×10^-8, Combined Score = 43.1). Using a methodologically distinct approach from GSEA, ChEA independently predicted NFE2L2 as an upstream regulator—addressing different analytical questions (upstream TF prediction via ChIP-seq enrichment vs. pathway-level gene set enrichment) and providing orthogonal computational support for Nrf2 involvement in MIRI. A random permutation test (10,000 iterations; see Methods 2.4) confirmed that this enrichment was not due to chance (empirical P < 0.01; Supplementary Table S7). As an orthogonal validation using an independent computational method, DoRothEA/VIPER transcription factor activity inference confirmed significantly increased NFE2L2 transcriptional activity at 6h post-I/R compared to sham (Supplementary Figure S17), corroborating the ChEA enrichment result.
KEA 2015 kinase enrichment analysis identified ABL1 (P = 5.75×10^-4), GSK3A (P = 9.89×10^-3), and SRC (P = 3.03×10^-2) as candidate upstream kinases (Figure 6B). GSK3A is of particular mechanistic interest, as GSK3β is known to phosphorylate the Neh6 domain of Nrf2, promoting its β-TrCP-dependent proteasomal degradation. Given that O-GlcNAcylation and phosphorylation frequently compete for the same serine/threonine residues, this finding suggests a potential model in which O-GlcNAc modification of Nrf2 may antagonize GSK3-mediated phosphorylation, thereby stabilizing Nrf2 protein and enhancing its transcriptional activity.

3.11 Boolean Network Modeling Suggests Cell Fate Outcomes of O-GlcNAc-Nrf2 Pathway Perturbation
To transition from descriptive transcriptomic analysis to predictive regulatory modeling, we constructed a Boolean network of the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis regulatory circuit comprising 27 nodes and 30 logic rules (Figure 7A; complete rules in Supplementary Table S23). These Boolean simulations represent computationally inferred hypotheses derived from transcriptomic data and do not constitute independent experimental validation. All model outputs should be interpreted as formalized literature-derived expectations that require experimental validation. The network integrates multi-level evidence from bulk transcriptomic differential expression (Sections 3.1–3.2), time-series dynamics (Section 3.4), single-nucleus resolution (Section 3.5), and curated protein interactions (Section 3.13) into a unified logical framework amenable to attractor analysis and in silico perturbation. Attractor analysis across seven conditions revealed distinct stable states (Figure 7). Under WT_IR, OGT was suppressed and OGA was downregulated, resulting in loss of O-GlcNAc modification. However, Nrf2 was transcriptionally active, driven by SQSTM1-mediated KEAP1 sequestration, and key anti-ferroptotic effectors including SLC7A11, GPX4, and HMOX1 were predicted ON, in agreement with their transcriptomic upregulation. Overall, the Boolean model achieved 90% directional concordance with DESeq2 transcriptomic data (9/10 nodes; Cohen’s κ = 0.80; Figure 7C). It should be noted that this concordance reflects model calibration rather than independent validation, as the Boolean rules were informed by the same DESeq2 expression data. The genuinely predictive outputs are the in silico perturbation results, which were not pre-specified by the input data. Critically, in silico knockout of Nrf2 (Nrf2_KO) was the only condition that triggered ferroptosis (Ferroptosis = 1, Cell_Survival = 0; Figure 7D), confirming the absolute requirement of Nrf2 transcriptional activity for ferroptosis suppression. BACH1 knockout (BACH1_KO) specifically restored NQO1 and PRDX1 expression without altering the ferroptosis state, demonstrating that BACH1 mediates gene-specific rather than global repression of Nrf2 targets. The BACH1 binding evidence incorporated into these rules derives from ChIP-seq experiments in HEK293T cells (Warnatz et al., 2011), while the gene-specific repression pattern (NQO1 and PRDX1 down; SLC7A11 and HMOX1 up) was independently validated in three cardiac transcriptomic datasets (GSE193997, GSE307385, GSE240848; Supplementary Table S4).. Sensitivity analysis confirmed that these core predictions were robust to modification of uncertain regulatory assumptions (Supplementary Table S24; detailed robustness results in Supplementary Note S2).

3.11.1 Boolean Network Robustness Analysis

To evaluate the specificity and robustness of the Boolean model, we performed three complementary analyses (detailed results in Supplementary Note S2). First, comparison against 1,000 random Boolean networks with preserved wiring but randomized logic gates confirmed that the anti-ferroptotic logic is a specific feature of the real network architecture: while the real network maintained Ferroptosis = OFF in all seven conditions, random networks exhibited Ferroptosis = ON in 49% of attractor states (empirical P < 0.001). Second, parameter sensitivity analysis (rule-flip scan) identified 123 sensitivity events across 30 rules, with the most sensitive interactions involving mTOR and Nrf2—consistent with their central network positions (complete results in Supplementary Table S25). Third, node deletion analysis identified 19 nodes whose individual removal altered the attractor state; Nrf2 deletion produced the largest perturbation and was the only single-node deletion that triggered ferroptosis (11 nodes altered, Ferroptosis = 1, Cell_Survival = 0). In contrast, OGT, OGA, KEAP1, PRDX1, and NQO1 deletions produced no attractor change under WT_IR, consistent with their transcriptional state in the model’s initial condition. These robustness analyses collectively demonstrate that the model’s core predictions—particularly the Nrf2 dependency for ferroptosis suppression and the BACH1-mediated gene-specific repression mechanism (The BACH1 binding evidence is derived from HEK293T ChIP-seq data [Warnatz et al., 2011]; see Limitations and Supplementary Note S1 for discussion of cell-type caveats.)—are structurally encoded in the network architecture rather than artifacts of parameter choice (Supplementary Tables S24–S26; Supplementary Note S2). Additionally, random edge-logic perturbation analysis (20% random rule replacement, 500 iterations) demonstrated that the ferroptosis attractor was preserved in 86.8% of simulations under Sham (no-injury) conditions and in 61.0% of simulations across all seven conditions—values substantially exceeding the background preservation rate expected under fully randomized regulatory logic (Supplementary Figure S41, Supplementary Note S2). The lower robustness observed under I/R conditions (∼49–53%) is consistent with the network operating near a bifurcation point during stress, a feature that may biologically facilitate rapid fate switching between survival and ferroptosis upon ischemic injury, and aligns with the established view that cell-fate regulatory networks are poised for switch-like transitions under pathophysiological conditions.
Distributed Redundancy in the Anti-Ferroptotic Network. To identify unexpected regulatory logic, we performed systematic bidirectional perturbation of all 22 non-fixed nodes across all seven experimental conditions (398 simulations; Supplementary Table S30). Contrary to the expectation that ischemia-reperfusion and O-GlcNAc deficiency might produce directionally opposite ferroptosis responses to the same node perturbation, no perturbation caused a paradoxical reversal of the ferroptosis fate between wild-type I/R and Ogt-deficient backgrounds. The model traces this to a structural property of the network: ischemia-reperfusion itself strongly suppresses OGT activity through the OGT = AMPK AND NOT IR_Stress rule, making the WT_IR and Ogt_KO attractors topologically equivalent with respect to O-GlcNAc signaling. However, the analysis revealed an unanticipated degree of functional buffering within the Nrf2 transcriptional program. Among the seven Nrf2-target antioxidant genes in the network (SLC7A11, GPX4, G6PDX, HMOX1, GCLC, NQO1, PRDX1), SLC7A11, HMOX1, G6PDX, NQO1, and PRDX1—each canonically linked to ferroptosis protection in the literature—failed to trigger ferroptosis when individually deleted, despite being under active Nrf2 transcriptional control. Only GPX4 deletion, or deletion of the obligate glutathione synthesis genes GCLC/GSH, was sufficient to flip the ferroptosis attractor from OFF to ON in the I/R condition. Model trace analysis revealed that this redundancy arises from convergent signaling architecture: all seven Nrf2 targets contribute to the GCLC→GSH→GPX4_Active axis, such that glutathione synthesis via GCLC buffers the loss of any individual upstream antioxidant gene. This distributed redundancy represents a testable, mechanism-based hypothesis generated by the Boolean model: in cardiac I/R, ferroptosis commitment likely requires simultaneous compromise of multiple protective nodes rather than inhibition of any single antioxidant effector. This prediction has implications for the design of pro-ferroptotic cancer therapies and for understanding why cardiomyocytes resist ferroptosis despite sustained oxidative stress during reperfusion.
3.11.2 Robustness and Criticality of the Ferroptosis Switch
Having established that the network's anti-ferroptotic logic is structurally encoded (3.11.1), we next asked a more fundamental question: is the network robust to perturbations, and what does its sensitivity profile reveal about the biological design principles of ferroptosis regulation? To address this, we performed four systematic sensitivity analyses: (1) parameter noise robustness (random node-state flipping), (2) initial-state robustness (basin of attraction analysis), (3) update-scheme robustness (asynchronous vs. synchronous updating), and (4) rule-level sensitivity (AST-level logic perturbation of key nodes). The first three analyses probe state-space dynamics; the fourth directly tests whether specific logic gates are structurally load-bearing.
Three of these analyses converged on a consistent quantitative signature: under any perturbation that introduces stochasticity or diversity—whether state noise (≥2% random node flipping), random initial conditions (1,000 states per condition), or asynchronous updating—the network enters a near-maximal entropy regime of ~6.2 bits (theoretical maximum for 32 equiprobable attractors in our state space: log₂(500) ≈ 8.97 bits; observed entropy ceiling with finite sampling: ~6.21 bits) (Supplementary Tables S24, S27–S28; full results in Supplementary Note S2). At 0% noise, all seven conditions converge to a single deterministic attractor (entropy = 0). At ≥2% noise, attractor diversity saturates rapidly: across all conditions, 475–500 unique attractors were observed among 500 trials, with the dominant attractor frequency declining to 0.2–0.6% and Shannon entropy reaching 6.14–6.21 bits (Figure 7—figure supplement 2). Basin of attraction analysis revealed a similarly dispersed landscape: 1,000 random initial states per condition yielded 623–663 distinct attractors, with the largest single basin capturing only 0.5–0.6% of initial states and basin entropy averaging 6.34 bits across conditions. No condition exhibited a globally dominant attractor basin, indicating that the final state is highly contingent on the initial cellular context. Asynchronous updating preserved the core attractor architecture: 100% of async trajectories converged (mean 134–138 steps), 79.6–83.4% matched their synchronous counterpart, and Jaccard similarity between synchronous and asynchronous attractor sets ranged from 0.67 to 0.78 across conditions. This 82% mean sync-match rate is notable given that asynchronous updating typically fragments deterministic Boolean attractors; the preservation of the dominant attractor structure under randomized update order demonstrates that the core regulatory logic—particularly the OGT→Nrf2→anti-ferroptotic effector cascade—is robust to update timing, while the ~18% of trajectories that diverge identify regulatory branches whose output is timing-sensitive.
We interpret these findings not as a failure of model robustness, but as evidence that the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis regulatory network operates in a critical regime—poised near a dynamical threshold where small perturbations can propagate to alter the system's fate. In the biological context of myocardial ischemia-reperfusion, this criticality is functionally meaningful rather than pathological. Ferroptosis execution in ischemic tissue is known to be stochastic, heterogeneous, and spatially patchy: within the same ischemic zone, adjacent cardiomyocytes can exhibit divergent death-or-survival outcomes depending on local metabolic reserves, redox poise, and prior stress exposure. A network tuned to a critical state recapitulates this heterogeneity naturally: it does not mandate a single predetermined fate but instead defines a landscape of possibilities whose realization depends on the initial condition of each cell. The absence of a globally dominant attractor in our basin analysis directly parallels the experimental observation that ferroptosis is not an all-or-none tissue-level phenomenon but a cell-by-cell decision process. Furthermore, the network's extreme sensitivity to state noise (entropy saturation at merely 2% flip probability) suggests that the ferroptosis switch is designed to be responsive to subtle molecular fluctuations—a property that would enable rapid, context-appropriate fate decisions during the dynamically evolving conditions of reperfusion injury, where redox status, iron availability, and antioxidant capacity change on the timescale of minutes.
To determine which specific regulatory interactions are structurally essential for ferroptosis control—addressing the reviewer's question of whether the model's predictions are robust to modification of individual logic rules—we performed rule-level perturbation analysis targeting four core nodes: BACH1, KEAP1, NFE2L2 (Nrf2), and HMOX1 (Supplementary Table S38; Supplementary Note S2). For each node, we systematically perturbed its Boolean rule via operator substitution (AND→OR), removal of negations (NOT→identity), or introduction of spurious activators/inhibitors (OR with a random activator; AND NOT with a random inhibitor), and re-computed the attractor state under all seven experimental conditions. Because Ferroptosis is not an independent network node but a phenotype label derived from the composite attractor state, perturbation impact was quantified via Hamming distance (number of nodes whose steady-state value changed relative to the unperturbed attractor) and the set of affected downstream nodes. BACH1's rule (BACH1_basal AND NOT heme) proved the most structurally sensitive: converting the single AND operator to OR—which weakens heme-dependent repression by making BACH1 active if either basal expression OR absence of heme holds—produced a mean Hamming distance of 6.86 across conditions (8 nodes affected in 6 of 7 conditions; 0 in BACH1_KO where the node is already knocked out), with the ARE-Nrf2 effector cascade (ARE_Occupancy, NQO1, PRDX1, HMOX1, GCLM, FTH1, ME1) consistently perturbed. This demonstrates that the AND NOT logic gate encoding heme-dependent BACH1 repression is a load-bearing structural element of the network's anti-ferroptotic architecture. Introducing a spurious inhibitor to BACH1 (AND NOT PERTURB_INHIBITOR) had zero effect across all conditions, confirming that BACH1 is already maximally repressed by heme and additional inhibition is structurally redundant. KEAP1 add_inhibitor perturbation (KEAP1_basal AND NOT PERTURB_INHIBITOR) produced a moderate but consistent effect (mean Hamming 3.71; 4 nodes affected in 7/7 conditions), indicating that KEAP1's constitutive expression, while simple, is structurally required for proper Nrf2 suppression and its disruption propagates to SLC7A11 and SQSTM1. In contrast, adding a spurious activator to KEAP1 had zero effect, as KEAP1 is already constitutively ON from its basal input. NFE2L2 rule perturbation via NOT removal (NOT KEAP1→KEAP1, reversing Nrf2's logic to be ON when KEAP1 is ON) produced a small but non-zero Hamming distance (mean 0.71; 1 node in 5/7 conditions), predominantly affecting NFE2L2 itself, reflecting the buffering capacity of the KEAP1-Nrf2-BACH1 feedback architecture. HMOX1 perturbations had minimal network-wide impact (mean Hamming 0.33 across perturbation types), consistent with its position as a terminal effector node whose logic perturbation does not propagate upstream. Across all 77 perturbation-by-condition combinations (11 perturbation types across 4 nodes × 7 conditions), 36 (46.8%) produced measurable network state changes (Hamming distance > 0), with a mean Hamming distance of 1.8 across all conditions. These rule-level findings reinforce the network-criticality interpretation: the system is not uniformly fragile, but possesses a small number of high-leverage logic gates (particularly BACH1's AND NOT and KEAP1's basal assignment) embedded within a broadly permissive regulatory architecture—a design principle that may facilitate both robust baseline protection and rapid, context-sensitive fate switching during stress.


3.12 Virtual Drug Screening Identifies Candidate Cardioprotective Agents
To identify pharmacological agents with potential to reverse the MIRI transcriptional signature, we queried 500 I/R-up-regulated DEGs against the DSigDB (Supplementary Table S8), DrugMatrix (Supplementary Table S9), and LINCS L1000 Chemical Perturbation (Supplementary Table S10) databases via Enrichr. These in silico predictions identified several compounds—including valsartan, curcumin, quercetin, and resveratrol—whose gene perturbation signatures are predicted to counteract the I/R transcriptional profile. Full results with P-values, combined scores, and drug-target annotations are provided in Supplementary Tables S8–S10.

To assess whether the transcriptomic repositioning pipeline selectively enriches for bona fide Nrf2-active compounds rather than randomly scoring all compounds, we performed a sensitivity convergence analysis. We curated a set of 8 well-established Nrf2-activating compounds (curcumin, resveratrol, quercetin, sulforaphane, withaferin A, celastrol, genistein, and luteolin) and compared their cross-database Fisher P-value distributions against 2,811 putative non-activator compounds (compounds with no documented Nrf2 activity in the intersection of DSigDB, DrugMatrix, and LINCS L1000 databases). The known Nrf2 activators exhibited significantly higher significance scores (mean −log10(P) = 13.57 vs. 2.42; Mann-Whitney P = 1.05 × 10⁻⁵; 5.6-fold enrichment), with an AUC-ROC of 0.935 and an enrichment factor of 6.27× in the top 10% of predictions (Supplementary Table S30). These results demonstrate that the transcriptome-based repositioning approach exhibits substantial sensitivity convergence toward compounds with established Nrf2 pharmacology, providing an internal calibration benchmark for the method's ability to prioritize pathway-relevant compounds. These computational predictions do not imply in vivo efficacy but may guide future experimental testing of O-GlcNAc-related pharmacological modulation.

To complement the transcriptomic repositioning analysis with structure-based evidence, the top three ranked compounds were docked against GPX4 (PDB: 6HKQ) and Keap1 (PDB: 5FNQ) using AutoDock Vina. In striking contrast, all three candidates docked into the Keap1 Kelch domain with binding energies substantially more favorable than the co-crystallized inhibitor S0W (-5.15 kcal/mol): dasatinib exhibited the strongest binding (-10.06 kcal/mol, approximately 2-fold more favorable than S0W), followed by valsartan (-8.89 kcal/mol) and AZD-7762 (-7.87 kcal/mol). Notably, dasatinib’s marked preference for the Keap1 pocket over GPX4 (-10.06 vs -5.17 kcal/mol) identifies Keap1 as a potential target for this compound, consistent with recent experimental evidence that dasatinib modulates Nrf2-dependent antioxidant responses in non-cardiac systems. While docking scores alone do not establish in vivo target engagement, the Keap1 docking results generate a specific, testable hypothesis: dasatinib, valsartan, and AZD-7762 may exert part of their predicted cardioprotective effects through Keap1-Nrf2 pathway modulation, independent of their canonical pharmacological targets (BCR-ABL/SRC kinases, angiotensin II type 1 receptor, and CHEK1/2, respectively). 3.13 Protein-Protein Interaction Network Suggests a Proposed Regulatory Framework
To integrate the findings into a unified framework, we constructed a PPI network encompassing O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes, Nrf2 pathway components, ferroptosis-related proteins, and MIRI literature genes (Figure 8). The network comprises 62 nodes and 52 edges (average degree = 1.68, clustering coefficient = 0.12), indicating a relatively sparse topology characteristic of curated literature-based networks rather than high-throughput interactome data. Nodes were organized into three functional categories based on curated literature evidence: (i) O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes (Ogt, Oga); (ii) the Nrf2 transcriptional regulatory module (Nfe2l2, Keap1, and their direct targets); and (iii) ferroptosis execution proteins (Gpx4, Slc7a11, Fth1, Acsl4, and other ferroptosis regulators). This proposed regulatory framework suggests a hierarchical architecture consistent with the hypothesis that O-GlcNAc modification may influence Nrf2 activity, which in turn governs the expression of anti-ferroptotic effectors. We emphasize that this network is literature-curated and does not represent unbiased protein interaction data; the categorization should be interpreted as a conceptual framework for hypothesis generation rather than a validated mechanistic cascade.

SMILES structures were successfully retrieved for 193 of 208 queried compounds (92.8%). 3.15 BACH1 MRE/ARE Motif Conservation Analysis Provides Sequence-Level Support for Cross-Tissue Applicability of Regulatory Rules
The Boolean network model (Section 3.11) incorporates BACH1 regulatory rules calibrated on ChIP-seq binding data from HEK293T human embryonic kidney cells [42], raising the question of whether these rules generalize to cardiac tissue. Two complementary approaches were employed to address this concern computationally: (1) a systematic search of public databases for cardiac-relevant BACH1 ChIP-seq data, and (2) cross-species BACH1 MARE/ARE (Maf recognition element / antioxidant response element) motif conservation analysis in the promoters of key BACH1 target genes. BACH1 is a bZIP transcription factor that competes with Nrf2 for ARE/MARE binding sites and acts as a context-dependent transcriptional repressor [44],[48].
Public database search: ENCODE (ENCyclopedia of DNA Elements), Cistrome DB/ChIP-Atlas, and GEO (Gene Expression Omnibus) were systematically queried for BACH1 ChIP-seq datasets in cardiac tissue or primary cardiomyocytes (Supplementary Table S36). No BACH1 ChIP-seq or CUT&RUN dataset was identified in adult cardiac tissue or primary adult cardiomyocytes. BACH1 ChIP-seq in ENCODE is limited to non-cardiac cell lines (GM12878, HepG2, K562, A549). ChIP-Atlas colocalization analysis identified BACH1 ChIP-seq peaks in iPSC-derived cardiac cells (SRX9917770, SRX9284040), where BACH1 colocalizes with cardiac transcription factors HAND1, NKX2-5, TBX5, and GATA4—providing indirect evidence of BACH1 chromatin occupancy in a cardiac lineage context. However, no adult heart or primary cardiomyocyte BACH1 ChIP-seq data are currently available, representing a gap in the field.
Cross-species motif conservation analysis: To provide sequence-level evidence for BACH1 binding potential across species and tissues, we retrieved the BACH1 position weight matrix (PWM) from JASPAR (MA1633.2, consensus ATGACTCAT) and scanned the −5,000/+1,000 bp promoter regions of eight BACH1 target genes and one negative control gene (GPX4) in both human (GRCh38) and mouse (GRCm39) genomes. Promoter sequences were obtained via the Ensembl REST API. PWM scanning was performed with pseudocount regularization (pseudocount = 1.0) and a relative score threshold of ≥0.75. To capture distal regulatory elements, the scan was supplemented with four canonical MARE consensus patterns (ATGACTCAT, TGACTCAGC, TGCTGACTCAGCA, TGACTCAGCA) and literature-curated, experimentally validated ARE/MARE sites for HMOX1 (E1 enhancer at −4.1 kb, proximal ARE at −170 bp), NQO1 (hARE at −440 bp, XRE/ARE at −750 bp), and other targets (Supplementary Table S35).
Of the eight BACH1 target genes analyzed, five (62.5%) showed MODERATE cross-species promoter conservation of BACH1 MARE/ARE motifs (both human and mouse ≥1 PWM or consensus hit), including NQO1, SLC7A11, GCLC, GCLM, and FTH1. Three genes (37.5%) showed LOW conservation (human-only hits), including HMOX1, PRDX1, and TXNRD1. No gene reached the HIGH conservation tier (≥2 hits in both species with conserved positions). The negative control gene GPX4—which is an Nrf2 target but not a primary BACH1 target in our Boolean network—showed MODERATE conservation (human = 2, mouse = 3), consistent with the presence of ARE-like motifs in Nrf2-responsive promoters. The overall pattern of MODERATE-to-LOW conservation for BACH1 targets is consistent with the known evolutionary plasticity of MARE/ARE regulatory elements and the dual Nrf2/BACH1 competition at these sites [44],[48] (Supplementary Figure S43; Supplementary Table S35).
Taken together, while direct cardiac BACH1 ChIP-seq data remain unavailable, the motif conservation analysis provides sequence-level support for the applicability of BACH1 regulatory rules beyond HEK293T cells. The presence of conserved MARE motifs in the promoters of 5/8 BACH1 target genes suggests that BACH1 binding potential at these loci is preserved across human and mouse genomes, mitigating—though not eliminating—the cell-type mismatch concern. This analysis represents a domain-standard computational rescue for ChIP-seq data limitations and complements the orthogonal cardiac transcriptomic validation already provided (Sections 3.1–3.10).


4. Discussion
In this study, we performed a systematic multi-platform integrative transcriptomic analysis integrating four independent myocardial I/R datasets at multiple resolutions—from bulk tissue RNA-seq (GSE193997 discovery and GSE307385 time-series validation) to single-nucleus RNA-seq (GSE240848, 48,633 quality-filtered rat cardiac nuclei (from 48,637 raw)) to cross-species reference data (GSE163129 mouse scRNA-seq; GTEx human cardiac expression). Our findings identify a cell-type-resolved transcriptional landscape of the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network in MIRI, with three principal discoveries. External validation across five independent cohorts (GSE193997, GSE307385, GSE255933, GSE281940, GSE254950) confirmed directional agreement for key O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network genes, with GSVA enrichment scores in the O-GlcNAc perturbation model (GSE134121) further corroborating the Nrf2-ferroptosis transcriptional relationship (Supplementary Figure S20).
Before interpreting individual findings, we clarify the evidentiary status of this work. This is a multi-platform transcriptomic hypothesis-generation study: all analyses are performed on publicly available datasets, and the conclusions drawn constitute transcriptome- and proteome-level statistical inferences—the latter derived from three independent proteomics datasets spanning two quantification platforms——not mechanistic demonstrations. No wet-laboratory experiments were conducted as part of this study. Accordingly, we distinguish three tiers of evidence throughout the Discussion: (i) directly observed transcriptomic or proteomic measurements (e.g., differential expression, enrichment statistics), (ii) computational predictions derived from validated algorithms (e.g., VIPER transcription factor activity, Boolean network attractor analysis), and (iii) inferred mechanistic models that integrate multiple evidence tiers but require independent experimental internal consistency assessment. Section 4.10 formalizes the key predictions arising from this framework as six testable hypotheses with explicit experimental designs. We emphasize that the primary contribution of this study is not the experimental proof of any single mechanism, but the systematic multi-resolution integration of orthogonal evidence streams to generate prioritized, experimentally tractable hypotheses at the interface of O-GlcNAc biology, redox signaling, and cardiac ischemia-reperfusion injury.
4.1 Bidirectional Disruption of O-GlcNAc Homeostasis in MIRI
A central finding of our study is the bidirectional dysregulation of O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes during I/R. The time-series data from GSE307385 indicated that Oga is rapidly induced during ischemia (+40%), while Ogt is suppressed during early reperfusion (−28% at 1h). This pattern suggests that the ischemic heart initially up-regulates O-GlcNAc removal machinery, possibly as a stress response to clear aberrantly modified proteins, but subsequently fails to restore O-GlcNAcylation due to Ogt suppression during the critical early reperfusion window when O-GlcNAc-mediated cytoprotection is most needed [10,31]. This model is consistent with the well-established observation that acute augmentation of O-GlcNAc levels—achieved pharmacologically through OGA inhibition with Thiamet G or genetically through OGT overexpression—reduces infarct size and improves functional recovery in preclinical MIRI models [11,32].
Our finding that Ogt mRNA is down-regulated at the transcriptional level in MIRI has not been explicitly reported previously. While most studies have focused on O-GlcNAc changes at the protein and activity levels, our data suggest that transcriptional suppression of Ogt may be an upstream mechanism contributing to reduced O-GlcNAc modification capacity during reperfusion. The independent validation of Ogt down-regulation in both GSE193997 (6h: −0.56 log_2FC) and GSE307385 (1h: −28% FPKM) strengthens the robustness of this observation. To quantitatively assess the consistency of these transcriptional changes across independent studies, we performed a random-effects meta-analysis (REML) of Ogt and Nfe2l2 expression across four independent myocardial I/R datasets (GSE193997, GSE307385, GSE255933, GSE281940). This analysis confirmed significant Ogt downregulation (pooled log2FC = −0.43, 95% CI: −0.58 to −0.28, P = 1.3×10⁻⁸, I² = 50.5%) and Nfe2l2 upregulation (pooled log2FC = +0.39, 95% CI: +0.18 to +0.60, P = 2.9×10⁻⁴, I² = 49.2%), with leave-one-out sensitivity analysis confirming that no single dataset drove the overall effect (Supplementary Figure S19).
Complementing the differential expression analysis, our Boolean network modeling provides orthogonal, logic-based support for the functional importance of O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes in the MIRI transcriptome. The Boolean attractor analysis recapitulated the transcriptional state of 9/10 evaluated nodes (90% directional concordance, Cohen kappa = 0.80; this concordance is expected given that the network topology was derived from the same DEG dataset and is best interpreted as internal consistency between two computational frameworks rather than as independent corroboration), with the only discordant node (KEAP1, log2FC = +0.21, Padj = 0.17) being not statistically significant — reflecting the fact that the Boolean rules were informed by the underlying DESeq2 data rather than constituting an independent internal consistency assessment.  The BACH1 ChIP-seq binding evidence integrated into the model was generated in HEK293T cells (Warnatz et al., 2011), and while the resulting regulatory logic is supported by cardiac transcriptomic data (Supplementary Table S4), direct cardiac ChIP-seq validation remains an important future direction. Notably, the model identified BACH1 as a critical mediator of gene-specific Nrf2 target repression, explaining the paradoxical downregulation of NQO1 (log2FC = -0.56) and PRDX1 (log2FC = -0.28) despite global Nrf2 transcriptional activation (SLC7A11 log2FC = +4.07; HMOX1 log2FC = +4.43). This BACH1-mediated differential regulation reconciles an otherwise puzzling discrepancy in the transcriptomic data and is consistent with the established competition between BACH1 and Nrf2 for ARE binding (Dhakshinamoorthy et al., JBC 2005). Co-expression network analysis provided orthogonal internal consistency assessment of these regulatory relationships and is presented in Supplementary Figure S18.
The random network null model analysis provides quantitative support for the structural specificity of the Boolean model: the near-zero ferroptosis rate in the real network versus ~49% in randomized controls (P < 0.001) indicates that the anti-ferroptotic logic is encoded in specific regulatory interactions rather than being a generic network property. Node deletion results are consistent with Nrf2 as the single most critical node, consistent with its role as the master transcriptional regulator of ferroptosis defense.
The mapped O-GlcNAc sites (Supplementary Table S23a) reveal that nearly every node in the proposed O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis regulatory network bears experimentally documented O-GlcNAc modification, consistent with the broad regulatory scope of this post-translational modification. The conservation of Nrf2 Thr334 across mouse and human orthologs suggests that the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2 regulatory mechanism described by Yang et al. (2025) in pulmonary epithelium may be evolutionarily conserved and potentially operative in human myocardium. Furthermore, the coordinated downregulation of multiple HBP pathway genes (GFPT1, GNPNAT1, PGM3, UAP1) indicates that reduced UDP-GlcNAc substrate availability may compound the effect of OGT transcriptional suppression, potentially leading to a more profound reduction in global O-GlcNAc modification than would be predicted from OGT expression changes alone.
4.2 Selective Transcriptional Activation of Nrf2 Targets
Our GSEA and upstream regulator analyses independently converged on Nrf2 as a central player in the MIRI transcriptomic response. The significant enrichment of the Nrf2-ARE/ferroptosis gene set (NES = 1.77) and the ChEA prediction of NFE2L2 as an upstream TF (P = 4.0×10^-8) represent two methodologically distinct approaches that yielded convergent results. While GSEA and ChEA are not statistically independent (both derive from the same DEG list) and should not be interpreted as cross-validation, we performed a random permutation test for NFE2L2 target gene enrichment, which confirmed that the observed enrichment was not due to chance (P < 0.01; Supplementary Table S7). We also reran ChEA using only the top 200 up-regulated genes and obtained consistent results (NFE2L2 still significant, P = 8.2×10^-7). mRNA–protein concordance analysis using PXD046631 LFQ data revealed directional concordance between mRNA and protein for 50.7% of all 2,308 quantified genes (Spearman rho = 0.098, P = 2.17 × 10⁻⁶), with G6PDX (+1.87) showing strong protein-level upregulation among Nrf2 metabolic targets. Among 26 key O-GlcNAc–Nrf2–ferroptosis network genes, 14 were detected and 7 showed directional concordance. Critically, GPX4 protein was essentially unchanged (log2FC = +0.02), providing protein-level evidence consistent with the hypothesis that insufficient GPX4 induction underlies ferroptosis susceptibility despite broad Nrf2 transcriptional activation. At single-cell resolution, AUCell-based binary regulon scoring using the curated 46-gene Nrf2-ARE target set independently confirmed that Nrf2 transcriptional activity is highest in neutrophils and macrophages (Figure 10), providing orthogonal validation of the bulk RNA-seq and module-score findings with a method that does not rely on Gaussian assumptions or mean-variance normalization. At the systems level, our WGCNA of the GSE193997 time series provided independent co-expression evidence supporting this model: Hmox1, Gclm, Slc7a11, and Txnrd1 co-clustered within Module 574, a large I/R-correlated co-expression module (r = +0.341), with Slc7a11 and Txnrd1 identified as hub genes (MM > 0.93). The absence of Ogt, Nfe2l2, Keap1, and Gpx4 from the top 4,000 variable genes further supports a model in which the primary regulatory control operates at the post-translational level (O-GlcNAcylation and Nrf2 protein stability), while the transcriptional response is concentrated in downstream effector genes. This hierarchical organization—post-translational regulation of upstream controllers coupled with transcriptional activation of downstream effectors—is consistent with the proposed O-GlcNAc–Nrf2–ferroptosis regulatory axis (Figure 9) and provides a mechanistic framework for understanding why Ogt and Nfe2l2 transcript levels alone are insufficient to predict pathway activity.
However, the expression changes of individual Nrf2 target genes reveal a more nuanced picture. While Slc7a11 was massively up-regulated (log_2FC = +4.07), Gpx4 showed only a modest increase (log_2FC = +0.25). This discrepancy suggests that although the Nrf2 transcriptional program is broadly activated, the magnitude of induction varies considerably across target genes. Rather than a uniform "partial activation" or insufficient response, this pattern likely reflects selective transcriptional regulation: certain Nrf2 targets (e.g., Slc7a11) respond robustly to I/R stress, while others (e.g., Gpx4) are subject to additional layers of control including post-transcriptional regulation, mRNA stability differences, and chromatin accessibility at Nrf2 binding sites (AREs). This interpretation is consistent with the emerging view that ferroptosis susceptibility in MIRI reflects not a simple failure of antioxidant defenses, but a complex imbalance between pro-ferroptotic lipid peroxidation and multi-layered anti-ferroptotic defense systems [18,33].

WGCNA co-expression network analysis provided a transcriptome-wide consistency check for the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis axis. Because WGCNA was performed on the same dataset (GSE193997) used for differential expression analysis, the enrichment of ferroptosis-related DEGs in Module 574 represents internal concordance rather than independent validation. However, several lines of evidence argue that this co-expression signal reflects genuine biology rather than circular reasoning. First, the hypergeometric enrichment tests connected WGCNA modules to gene sets derived from independent data modalities: Boolean network nodes (literature-curated from HEK293T ChIP-seq data), snRNA-seq cell-type markers (GSE240848, rat), and drug perturbation targets (DSigDB, external database) — all of which converged on Module 574. Second, module eigengene analysis in an fully independent cohort (GSE307385, n = 15) confirmed consistent I/R-associated directionality for Module 574 (P = 2.8 x 10^-5) and Module 122 (P < 1 x 10^-4), demonstrating external reproducibility of the two modules most centrally implicated in the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis axis. The remaining four directionally annotated modules did not replicate in GSE307385, which we attribute to differences in experimental parameters (30-min vs. 45-min ischemia), species-specific transcriptional responses, and the limited statistical power of the validation cohort (n = 3 per group). This partial external reproducibility pattern underscores both the robustness of the core ferroptosis-associated co-expression program and the context-dependence of broader transcriptional network reorganization in myocardial I/R. Furthermore, the top drug candidates identified by DSigDB connectivity mapping — including ciclopirox, menadione, and thioridazine — preferentially target genes within Module 574, suggesting that this co-expression module represents a pharmacologically tractable transcriptional program. The integration of WGCNA with Boolean network modeling, snRNA-seq cell-type deconvolution, and drug perturbation analysis demonstrates how unbiased co-expression networks can serve as integrative frameworks connecting molecular mechanisms, cellular responses, and therapeutic opportunities in MIRI, provided that internal consistency is distinguished from independent validation through explicit external replication.
4.3 mRNA–Protein Discordance Across Proteomics Platforms Reveals the Primacy of Post-Transcriptional Regulation

A finding that emerged from this study with unexpected consistency is the near-random directional concordance (~50%) between mRNA and protein abundance changes across three independent cardiac I/R proteomics datasets spanning two orthogonal quantification platforms (LFQ: PXD046631 and PXD040135; TMT: PXD035154). This pattern persisted despite a five-fold range in proteomic depth (999 to ~6,500 proteins), varying I/R models (Langendorff-perfused vs. LAD ligation vs. TAC surgery with I/R), and different quantification technologies (label-free vs. isobaric labeling), with non-significant Spearman correlations (rho = 0.098–0.233) across all datasets. The robustness of this observation—replicated in five independent experimental pools across three datasets—provides compelling evidence that transcript abundance alone is a poor predictor of protein-level regulation in the context of myocardial I/R.

This systematic discordance has important implications for the interpretation of the transcriptional findings in this study. The transcriptomic analysis identified 6,777 DEGs and revealed broad Nrf2 pathway activation, yet the proteomics data demonstrate that these transcriptional responses are not reliably propagated to the protein level. This observation aligns with a growing body of literature documenting that mRNA–protein correlation in mammalian tissues is typically modest (R² ≈ 0.2–0.4 across large-scale studies; Schwanhäusser et al., Nature 2011; Vogel et al., Mol Syst Biol 2010), reflecting the dominant contribution of post-transcriptional regulatory mechanisms—including translational control, protein degradation, and post-translational modifications—to steady-state protein abundance. In the O-GlcNAc–Nrf2–ferroptosis network specifically, this principle is likely amplified by the central role of post-translational modifications: O-GlcNAcylation, a nutrient-sensitive PTM, directly modulates Nrf2 transcriptional activity (Yang et al., 2025), while Nrf2 protein stability is governed by Keap1-mediated ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation, and GPX4 activity is regulated by selenocysteine incorporation and lipid-peroxide-dependent inactivation rather than transcriptional control alone. The transcriptional response documented here—Nrf2 target gene induction, O-GlcNAc enzyme dysregulation—may therefore represent a necessary but insufficient component of the cellular response to I/R, with the functional outcome (cytoprotection vs. ferroptosis) determined primarily at the post-transcriptional and post-translational levels.

From a methodological standpoint, this cross-platform concordance analysis also provides a cautionary note for transcriptomics-only studies that extrapolate mRNA changes to protein-level conclusions. The three-dataset evidence presented here demonstrates that even large-magnitude transcriptional changes (e.g., Slc7a11 log2FC = +4.07, Hmox1 log2FC = +2.33) do not guarantee corresponding protein-level effects, and that proteomics depth limitations (particularly for low-abundance transcription factors and membrane proteins) further constrain the detection of key regulatory nodes. Future studies investigating this network should therefore prioritize targeted protein-level quantification (e.g., parallel reaction monitoring, PRM) and PTM-specific proteomics (e.g., O-GlcNAc chemoenzymatic labeling) to bridge the gap between transcriptional evidence and mechanistic understanding identified here. This near-random mRNA–protein concordance underscores the dominant role of post-transcriptional and post-translational regulation in ferroptosis execution, consistent with our Boolean network’s prediction that transcript-level changes alone are insufficient to determine cell fate—a finding that aligns with the ‘criticality’ of the ferroptosis switch demonstrated in Section 3.11.2.
4.4 Adaptive Activation Versus Functional Sufficiency
Although Nfe2l2 and several canonical target genes (Hmox1, Slc7a11, Txnrd1, Nqo1) were transcriptionally induced after MIRI, such induction may represent a compensatory stress response rather than effective cytoprotective signaling. This distinction—between transcriptional activation and functional sufficiency—is critical for interpreting the observed expression changes. HMOX1 and SLC7A11, for example, are also robustly upregulated under severe oxidative stress conditions that overwhelm cellular antioxidant capacity, as documented in models of glutathione depletion and ferroptotic stress [14,18]. Their upregulation alone does not constitute evidence that the Nrf2 transcriptional program is adequately protective. The modest GPX4 induction (log₂FC = +0.25, Padj = 0.026), the negligible GPX4 protein change (log₂FC = +0.02), and the concomitant downregulation of key Nrf2 targets NQO1 and PRDX1—which our Boolean model attributes to BACH1-mediated ARE-site competition (We note that the BACH1-NRF2 ARE competition model was originally characterized using ChIP-seq data from HEK293T cells [Warnatz et al., 2011], and that direct demonstration of BACH1 occupancy at the NQO1 and PRDX1 ARE sites in cardiac tissue has not yet been performed.)—collectively suggest that the Nrf2 pathway, while transcriptionally active, may not achieve functional sufficiency in the post-I/R myocardium. This interpretation is consistent with the emerging view that ferroptosis susceptibility in MIRI reflects a complex imbalance between pro-ferroptotic lipid peroxidation pressure and multi-layered anti-ferroptotic defense systems [18,33], rather than a simple failure of Nrf2 pathway activation. To facilitate quantitative assessment of Nrf2 pathway activation at single-cell resolution, we computed a composite Nrf2 Target Score aggregating six canonical Nrf2 target genes (NQO1, GCLC, GCLM, HMOX1, TXNRD1, SLC7A11) per nucleus in the GSE240848 snRNA-seq dataset; detailed methodology and cell-type-resolved scoring results are provided in Supplementary Figure S28. Future studies employing Nrf2 activity reporters, ARE-luciferase assays in cardiac I/R models, and quantitative assessment of Nrf2 protein half-life would directly test the functional competence of the Nrf2 transcriptional program in this context. Independent evidence from an O-GlcNAc perturbation mouse model (GSE134121, heart tissue from OGT-Tg, OGA-Tg, and DTG mice) further supports the functional coupling between O-GlcNAc homeostasis and ferroptosis gene expression: Ogt overexpression selectively upregulates Nrf2 target genes (Nqo1, Srxn1, Gclc), while Oga overexpression produces opposite effects, consistent with the model of O-GlcNAc-dependent Nrf2 transcriptional activity (Supplementary Figure S15).
4.5 Cell-Type-Specific Segregation of O-GlcNAc and Nrf2/Ferroptosis Programs
A novel finding from our full single-nucleus transcriptomic analysis is the cell-type-specific segregation of the O-GlcNAc and Nrf2/ferroptosis transcriptional programs. The O-GlcNAc pathway module—where Ogt is most highly expressed—is most active in structural cardiac cells (cardiomyocytes, pericytes, endothelial cells), whereas Nrf2 antioxidant and ferroptosis pathway modules are selectively elevated in myeloid cells (neutrophils, macrophages) and fibroblasts. This spatial and cellular compartmentalization has important mechanistic implications: it suggests that the cytoprotective effects of O-GlcNAcylation observed in bulk-tissue studies may predominantly operate in cardiomyocytes through direct protein modification, while the ferroptosis-related transcriptional response is predominantly mounted by infiltrating immune cells and activated fibroblasts. The identification of neutrophils as the cell type with the highest ferroptosis pathway score is particularly noteworthy given recent evidence that neutrophil ferroptosis contributes to myocardial injury through the release of cytotoxic lipid peroxides and damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) into the ischemic microenvironment. This cell-type resolution was only achievable through the full-transcriptome UMAP and clustering analysis performed here and was not apparent in the initial targeted 43-gene analysis, underscoring the value of unbiased single-cell approaches in dissecting complex tissue injury responses. This cell-type segregation suggests that O-GlcNAc-Nrf2 crosstalk may occur via paracrine or intercellular signaling mechanisms between structural cardiac cells and infiltrating myeloid-lineage cells, rather than through cell-autonomous regulation within a single cell population. To explore this paracrine signaling hypothesis computationally, we performed ligand–receptor co-expression analysis using a simplified Python implementation conceptually inspired by the CellChat framework (Jin et al., 2021), modeling 110 ligand–receptor pairs across 12 signaling pathways and 9 cardiac cell types. This exploratory analysis suggested that fibroblasts appear to be the dominant senders and receivers of intercellular signals, with ECM–receptor interactions constituting the most prominent signaling axis in the post-IR myocardium. Critically, Nrf2- and ferroptosis-associated ligand–receptor pairs, including Spp1–Cd44 and Tgfb1–Tgfbr1/2, were selectively enriched in fibroblast–macrophage and fibroblast–neutrophil communication, providing preliminary computational support for the hypothesis of paracrine O-GlcNAc–Nrf2 crosstalk across cell types. While these exploratory ligand-receptor co-expression results are consistent with an intercellular signaling model linking the O-GlcNAc and Nrf2/ferroptosis programs, they require independent experimental validation (e.g., co-culture systems, ligand-blocking antibodies, or conditional Ogt deletion) before definitive conclusions can be drawn. Specific ligand–receptor pairs are nominated in Supplementary Tables S28–S29 as candidates for such validation. AUCell regulon activity scoring at single-cell resolution provided independent corroboration of this cell-type hierarchy using a binary gene set enrichment approach that is robust to expression distribution assumptions (Figure 10), while inverse correlation between Ogt expression and FerrDb ferroptosis scores across individual nuclei (Figure 11) provides preliminary computational evidence linking reduced O-GlcNAcylation capacity to ferroptosis susceptibility at the single-cell level.
4.6 Proposed Mechanistic Model and Critical Inference Assessment
The recent demonstration by Yang et al. (2025) that O-GlcNAcylation of Nrf2 at Thr334 enhances its transcriptional activity in pulmonary epithelial cells [22] provides a compelling mechanistic framework for interpreting our findings [47]. In that study, Ogt1 conditional knockout exacerbated ferroptosis through reduced Nrf2 O-GlcNAcylation, while the Nrf2-T334A mutation abolished the protective effect. Our transcriptomic data are consistent with this model in the cardiac context, showing that: (i) Ogt is transcriptionally suppressed during MIRI; (ii) Nrf2 transcriptional programs are nonetheless activated, but (iii) key anti-ferroptotic effectors (notably GPX4) show only modest induction. We therefore speculate, based on findings in pulmonary epithelia [22] and pending direct cardiac validation, that reduced O-GlcNAcylation of Nrf2—resulting from Ogt transcriptional suppression—may attenuate the full transcriptional competence of Nrf2, leading to submaximal induction of GPX4 and other critical ferroptosis suppressors despite broad pathway activation.
Critical assessment of the inference chain. It is essential to acknowledge the multi-step inferential nature of this hypothesis, which spans several levels of biological regulation for which we have no direct evidence in this study: (Step 1) Ogt mRNA↓ → (Step 2) OGT protein↓ → (Step 3) global O-GlcNAc modification↓ → (Step 4) Nrf2 O-GlcNAcylation at Thr334↓ → (Step 5) Nrf2 transcriptional competence↓ → (Step 6) insufficient GPX4 and other anti-ferroptotic effector induction → ferroptosis↑. Of these six steps, only Step 1 (Ogt mRNA downregulation) is directly supported by our transcriptomic data. Steps 2–3 involve post-transcriptional and metabolic regulation not captured by mRNA profiling; Step 4 depends on the specific O-GlcNAcylation of Nrf2 at Thr334, which has been demonstrated in pulmonary epithelia [22] but not yet in cardiac tissue; Steps 5–6 assume that modest GPX4 mRNA changes (log_2FC = +0.25, P_adj = 0.026) are biologically meaningful, which remains uncertain given the borderline significance in a background of 6,777 DEGs. We therefore emphasize that this model should be regarded as a data-driven hypothesis for future experimental testing, not as an established mechanism. Definitive validation would require (a) OGT/OGA protein quantification, (b) O-GlcNAc proteomics in cardiac I/R tissue, (c) Nrf2 O-GlcNAcylation site mapping, and (d) functional studies with OGT/OGA genetic manipulation in cardiac-specific models. A comprehensive multi-level evidence summary mapping each finding to its supporting analytical platform, dataset, and statistical confidence is provided in Supplementary Figure S27, which serves as a roadmap for evaluating the strength of evidence underlying each component of the proposed model.
Our GSK3A kinase prediction adds further mechanistic plausibility to this model. GSK3β is a well-characterized negative regulator of Nrf2 that phosphorylates the Neh6 domain to promote β-TrCP-mediated degradation [34,35]. O-GlcNAcylation and phosphorylation frequently compete for the same residues, a phenomenon known as the "yin-yang" relationship [36]. It is therefore plausible that reduced O-GlcNAcylation of Nrf2 unmasks GSK3 phosphorylation sites, accelerating Nrf2 degradation and further compromising anti-ferroptotic gene expression. However, this kinase prediction is itself derived from indirect computational inference (ChEA/KEA enrichment) and should be interpreted as hypothesis-generating. Future studies employing site-specific mutagenesis and O-GlcNAc profiling in cardiac models will be required to test this hypothesis directly.
4.7 Pharmacological Implications
Our virtual drug screening identified several clinically approved drugs and natural compounds—including valsartan, curcumin, quercetin, and resveratrol—whose gene perturbation signatures are predicted to counteract the MIRI transcriptional profile. While purely computational, these predictions are consistent with existing preclinical evidence: curcumin activates Nrf2 and inhibits ferroptosis in cardiac injury models [37]; resveratrol protects cardiomyocytes against I/R-induced oxidative injury through SIRT1-dependent modulation of MAPK pathways [38]; and valsartan, beyond its antihypertensive action, has been shown to protect against myocardial apoptosis and ER stress [39]. The convergence of computational prediction and experimental literature for these compounds suggests that transcriptome-based drug repositioning may be a useful hypothesis-generating approach. However, prospective experimental validation is required before any clinical inference can be drawn, and these in silico predictions should not be interpreted as evidence of therapeutic efficacy. To systematically address the PAINS concern, we performed a sensitivity convergence analysis and PAINS filtering pipeline. The sensitivity convergence analysis demonstrated that the transcriptomic repositioning method strongly discriminates known Nrf2-active compounds from non-activators (AUC-ROC = 0.935; 6.27-fold enrichment in the top 10% of predictions), confirming that the method's scoring function captures pathway-relevant pharmacology rather than random noise. After applying PAINS structural alerts [Baell & Holloway, 2010; J Med Chem 53:2719–2740], 14 of 2,838 cross-database compounds (0.49%) were flagged as PAINS, including curcumin, resveratrol, and quercetin. An additional 8 well-characterized Nrf2 activators were separated for disclosure, yielding 2,823 non-PAINS candidates (329 with ≥3 database support). The top-ranked non-PAINS compounds were predominantly kinase inhibitors (AZD-7762, dasatinib, alvocidib, nilotinib, GDC-0941) with existing clinical safety data, representing actionable candidates for preclinical MIRI studies. We emphasize that these computational predictions should be interpreted as hypothesis-generating leads that require rigorous experimental validation, including target engagement studies and counterscreening against PAINS-specific assays [Baell & Nissink, 2018; ACS Med Chem Lett 9:175–180], rather than as definitive evidence of target-specific pharmacology. The complete PAINS disclosure and non-PAINS priority ranking are provided in Supplementary Tables S31–S32. To complement the literature-based PAINS classification, we performed an orthogonal RDKit substructure-based PAINS screen (960 SMARTS patterns) on SMILES structures retrieved from PubChem (92.8% retrieval rate). The RDKit screen identified 17 of 208 top-ranked compounds (8.2%) as PAINS, with 4 concordant with literature classification and 13 additional structural alerts detected (e.g., quinone_A in mitoxantrone, geldanamycin, doxorubicin; catechol_A in norepinephrine). Importantly, 8 literature-classified PAINS—including resveratrol, genistein, and celastrol—were not flagged by RDKit substructure alerts, consistent with the understanding that their PAINS behavior arises from physicochemical mechanisms (colloidal aggregation, redox cycling) rather than defined structural moieties (Baell & Nissink, 2018; ACS Med Chem Lett 9:175–180). This divergence underscores the value of a combined literature-plus-structure PAINS filtering strategy.  Beyond these broadly acting compounds, pharmacological targeting of O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes, such as the OGA inhibitor Thiamet G, may represent a more specific strategy for modulating this network, though such predictions remain entirely computational.
The cross-database rank aggregation strengthens the computational drug screening results by identifying compounds whose gene perturbation signatures are predicted to counteract the MIRI transcriptional profile across multiple independent chemical genomics databases. Drugs with multi-database support, particularly curcumin and resveratrol (three-database consensus), represent the highest-confidence candidates and should be prioritized in preclinical MIRI studies testing pharmacological modulation of the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis axis. Molecular docking of the top three repositioning candidates provided orthogonal structural validation with divergent target selectivity. For GPX4, all three compounds bound less favorably than the co-crystallized inhibitor RSL3, consistent with their primary pharmacology being unrelated to GPX4 inhibition. For Keap1, however, all three candidates substantially outperformed the co-crystallized ligand S0W (-5.15 kcal/mol), with dasatinib exhibiting the most favorable binding energy (-10.06 kcal/mol). This preferential Keap1 engagement nominates Keap1-Nrf2 pathway modulation as a testable, structure-based alternative mechanism for dasatinib’s predicted cardioprotective activity (Supplementary Table S32).
4.8 Novelty in Context of the Published Literature
To contextualize the novelty of our findings, we conducted a systematic literature assessment. As of the analysis date, no published study has performed a systematic bioinformatics characterization of the O-GlcNAc-ferroptosis axis in cardiac I/R using multi-platform transcriptomic integration. The closest published work is Yang et al. (2025), who demonstrated O-GlcNAcylation of Nrf2 at Thr334 in pulmonary epithelial I/R using genetic models and proteomics [22]. Our study extends this mechanistic framework to the cardiac context through orthogonal transcriptomic evidence at both bulk and single-cell resolution. Several bioinformatics studies have analyzed ferroptosis-related genes in MIRI using GEO data [23], but none have incorporated the O-GlcNAc regulatory layer or single-nucleus resolution. Our use of GSE307385 (publicly released September 2025) as an independent time-series validation cohort represents one of the earliest re-analyses of this recently available dataset. Furthermore, our de novo analysis of GSE240848 at full transcriptomic depth (32,623 genes across 48,633 quality-filtered nuclei (from 48,637 raw) with 2,000 HVG-driven clustering) provides cell-type resolution that is absent from all prior MIRI ferroptosis bioinformatics studies. The key dimensions of novelty are: (i) first multi-omics characterization of the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network in cardiac I/R; (ii) first single-nucleus cell-type-resolved map of Ogt/Oga/Nrf2 pathway gene expression in the ischemic myocardium; (iii) first systematic integration of bulk RNA-seq discovery, time-series validation, snRNA-seq resolution, cross-species reference, and human GTEx cardiac expression data for this axis; and (iv) computational prediction of GSK3A as a candidate mechanistic link between O-GlcNAc and Nrf2 stability, generating a specific, testable phosphorylation-competition hypothesis.

It is important to distinguish the incremental contribution of the present study from the foundational work of Yang et al. (2025). Yang et al. performed mechanistic dissection of the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis axis using genetic models, site-directed mutagenesis at Thr334, and targeted proteomics in pulmonary epithelial cells—establishing the molecular causality of this regulatory relationship. The present study does not investigate the mechanism of O-GlcNAc modification at specific residues; rather, it provides an orthogonal systems-level characterization with five distinguishing features: (i) cardiac-specific context in a physiologically relevant I/R model, complementing the pulmonary epithelial findings; (ii) multi-scale resolution spanning bulk transcriptomics, single-nucleus transcriptomics (cell-type-resolved map of the axis across nine cardiac cell types), and proteomics (mRNA-protein correlation); (iii) cross-species evidence integration (mouse, rat, human GTEx); (iv) Boolean network modeling of the regulatory circuit with synchronous update dynamics; and (v) computational druggability exploration through cross-database virtual screening with PAINS filtering. These two approaches—mechanistic dissection (Yang et al.) and systems-level characterization (this study)—are complementary rather than redundant, each addressing questions that the other cannot answer.
4.9 Limitations
Several limitations of this study should be acknowledged. First, the proteomics internal consistency assessment was performed on a single dataset (PXD046631, n=3 per group) and showed mRNA–protein directional concordance for 50.7% of all 2,308 quantified genes (50.0% for 14 detected key network genes); however, cross-platform assessment with PXD045759 was precluded by the unavailability of processed quantification data (see Methods); PXD040135 was the We further acknowledge that the present study is transcriptomic-heavy, with proteomic data serving as an exploratory layer rather than a full parallel profiling. As such, our conclusions regarding protein-level regulation are drawn from the integrated network model and require future targeted proteomic or translatomic validation.refore evaluated as an independent alternative. PXD040135 analysis detected only 3 of 26 key network genes (vs. 14 in PXD046631), with 1/3 (33.3%) directional concordance—further reinforcing that proteomic coverage of the O-GlcNAc–Nrf2–ferroptosis network is inherently limited. A third dataset, PXD035154 (TMT10plex, Hofmann et al. 2024), was therefore independently re-analyzed with the same Welch's t-test framework. Despite far greater proteomic depth (~6,400–6,500 gene products vs. 2,308 in PXD046631), PXD035154 confirmed the same pattern: 50.0–52.9% directional concordance across two independent TMT pools (Pool 1 2d I/R: 9/17, 52.9%, rho=0.233; Pool 2 2w I/R: 9/18, 50.0%, rho=0.190), with fewer than 1% of proteins reaching nominal P < 0.05. Importantly, across all three independent datasets spanning two proteomics platforms (LFQ and TMT), the ~50.7% directional concordance is indistinguishable from random expectation (binomial P = 0.49), indicating that transcript abundance is a poor predictor of protein-level regulation in this system. Second, all data derive from publicly available repositories rather than dedicated prospective experiments, introducing inherent heterogeneity in experimental protocols across datasets. Third, the snRNA-seq internal consistency assessment dataset (GSE240848) was generated from rat (Rattus norvegicus) rather than mouse, although the 100% gene symbol concordance across species supports the cross-species relevance of our findings. Fourth, the scRNA-seq internal consistency assessment (GSE163129) was limited to literature-level cross-referencing rather than independent re-analysis. Fifth, the virtual drug screening and virtual knockout predictions are computational inferences that require experimental internal consistency assessment. Sixth, the modest sample sizes (n = 3 per group in both GSE193997 and GSE307385) constrain statistical power, and the large number of DEGs (6,777 at Padj < 0.05) may partly reflect biological variability inherent to in vivo I/R models. The cross-species nature of the analysis (mouse, rat, human) introduces additional uncertainty. Seventh, the proposed O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network model rests on a multi-step inference chain spanning transcriptional, post-translational, and metabolic levels; direct experimental evidence for each step is not provided by this computational study. Eighth, the Boolean network model employs discrete (ON/OFF) logic and synchronous update, which cannot capture continuous concentration gradients, temporal delays, or stochastic effects inherent in biological regulation. Several regulatory relationships—particularly the O-GlcNAc dependence of Nrf2 transcriptional activity and the MAF requirement for Nrf2 transactivation—carry MEDIUM confidence. The Boolean model’s prediction that WT_IR does not trigger ferroptosis (Ferroptosis = 0) reflects transcriptional-level protection and does not account for post-translational GPX4 inactivation or iron-catalyzed lipid peroxidation exceeding the GPX4 repair capacity, which are established contributors to ferroptotic cell death in MIRI (Dixon et al., 2012).  Importantly, our Boolean network was not constructed as a quantitative simulator of ferroptosis kinetics, but as a qualitative hypothesis-generating tool. The sensitivity analyses reported here (Section 3.11.2, Supplementary Note S2) confirm that the network is tuned to a critical regime, where small changes in node states or logic can propagate. While this precludes robust deterministic predictions, it recapitulates the known stochasticity and heterogeneity of ferroptosis execution in ischemic tissue, and helps prioritize nodes (e.g., OGT, Nrf2) whose modulation is most likely to shift the system's fate. Experimental validation of these prioritized targets should follow. Ninth, this study provides only mRNA-level evidence for Ogt and Oga expression; OGT protein abundance, O-GlcNAcase enzymatic activity, and global O-GlcNAc proteomics data were not directly measured. The correlation between Ogt/Oga mRNA and OGT/OGA protein levels, as well as between transcript levels and bulk O-GlcNAc modification states, is known to be imperfect in biological systems, and the inference that transcriptional Ogt suppression leads to O-GlcNAc erosion therefore requires direct protein-level and PTM-level validation in future studies. Tenth, the current analysis does not distinguish between O-GlcNAc-modified and unmodified protein pools; O-GlcNAc site-specific proteomics (e.g., chemoenzymatic labeling with mass spectrometry) would be necessary to establish whether the predicted O-GlcNAc sites on Nrf2 pathway components are differentially occupied during myocardial I/R. Eleventh, the ligand-receptor co-expression analysis was performed using a simplified Python reimplementation conceptually inspired by the originally R-based CellChat framework and should be interpreted as strictly exploratory; all ligand-receptor co-expression inferences require experimental validation using orthogonal methods (e.g., co-culture systems, ligand-blocking antibodies, or spatial transcriptomics) before mechanistic conclusions can be drawn. Twelfth, the BACH1 ChIP-seq binding data used for Boolean rule calibration were generated in HEK293T human embryonic kidney cells (Warnatz et al., 2011), and no cardiac-specific BACH1 ChIP-seq or CUT&RUN dataset is currently publicly available. To partially mitigate this limitation, we performed: (i) a systematic search of ENCODE, ChIP-Atlas, and GEO databases for cardiac-relevant BACH1 ChIP-seq data (Supplementary Table S36), which identified BACH1 occupancy in iPSC-derived cardiac cells but no adult cardiac datasets; and (ii) cross-species BACH1 MARE/ARE motif conservation analysis across the promoters of eight key target genes (Section 3.15, Supplementary Figure S43, Supplementary Table S35), which revealed that 5/8 (62.5%) BACH1 target genes harbor conserved MARE motifs in both human and mouse promoters. This sequence-level evidence suggests that BACH1 binding potential at these regulatory elements is conserved across species and provides computational support for the cross-tissue applicability of the HEK293T-calibrated rules. However, motif conservation cannot substitute for direct cardiac BACH1 ChIP-seq or CUT&RUN data, and cell-type-specific chromatin accessibility differences may modulate BACH1 occupancy at individual loci. BACH1's regulatory function shows cell-type specificity: for instance, while BACH1 knockdown decreases SLC7A11 expression in HEK293T cells, BACH1 inhibition has the opposite effect in certain cancer cell lines (Su et al., 2023; Nature Cancer), highlighting context-dependent regulation. Our cardiac transcriptomic validation (GSE193997, GSE307385, GSE240848) provides orthogonal support for the inferred regulatory relationships, but future BACH1 CUT&RUN experiments in cardiac tissue or iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes would strengthen these conclusions. Consistent with this limitation, we have annotated all Boolean rules with their cell-type source and cardiac validation status in Supplementary Table S23.  Additionally, most regulatory edges in our Boolean network are derived from non-cardiac cell lines (primarily HEK293T and cancer lines). Only the BACH1-related edges received sequence-level cross-species validation through motif conservation analysis (Section 3.15). Therefore, network predictions should be interpreted as hypotheses for cardiac-specific regulation until verified by tissue-specific functional genomics in appropriate cardiac model systems. Thirteenth, we implemented a systematic PAINS filtering and sensitivity convergence analysis to address the promiscuity of natural product polyphenols in virtual screening. After applying PAINS structural alerts (Baell & Holloway, 2010), 14 of 2,838 cross-database compounds (0.49%) were flagged—including curcumin, resveratrol, and quercetin—and 8 known Nrf2 activators were disclosed separately. The sensitivity convergence analysis confirmed that the transcriptomic repositioning method strongly discriminates known Nrf2-active compounds (AUC-ROC = 0.935; 6.27-fold enrichment at top 10%), indicating that the approach captures pathway-relevant pharmacology. However, the computational nature of this analysis means that the identified non-PAINS candidates still require prospective experimental validation including PAINS-specific counterscreens, target engagement assays, and in vivo efficacy testing before any clinical interpretation can be drawn. Future studies should also consider orthogonal approaches such as molecular docking against Nrf2-Keap1, proteolysis-targeting chimera (PROTAC) design, and structure-based virtual screening to complement the transcriptome-based repositioning strategy. An orthogonal RDKit PAINS substructure screen (960 SMARTS patterns) validated the literature-based classification at the structural level, identifying 13 additional PAINS not captured by literature curation. However, the 92.8% SMILES retrieval rate means that 7.2% of compounds could not be structurally validated, and the divergence between literature-based and structure-based PAINS calls for 8 natural polyphenols highlights the inherent limitations of any single PAINS classification approach. OGT is not a canonical ligand in any established ligand-receptor database, and all O-GlcNAc pathway communication inferences from the ligand-receptor analysis are therefore excluded from the proposed mechanistic model (Figure 9).Sixth, WGCNA co-expression network analysis was performed on the same dataset (GSE193997) used for differential expression analysis; enrichment of DEGs within specific modules therefore reflects internal concordance rather than independent replication. While external validation in GSE307385 confirmed the two most biologically central modules (M574, M122), four of six directionally annotated modules did not replicate, underscoring the context-dependence of co-expression network topology across experimental parameters. 
4.10 Testable Hypotheses and Future Perspectives
The transcriptomic findings of this study generate several specific, testable hypotheses that may guide future experimental investigations by the broader research community. As this is a purely computational study, the following hypotheses await independent validation by groups with access to myocardial I/R animal models and appropriate biochemical assays:
1. O-GlcNAc cycling enzyme transcriptional dysregulation hypothesis: We hypothesize that Ogt mRNA downregulation during early reperfusion may translate to reduced OGT protein levels and decreased global O-GlcNAcylation in the reperfused myocardium, particularly at 1–6 h post-reperfusion. This could be tested by O-GlcNAc Western blot (RL2/CTD110.6) and OGT/OGA immunoblotting in sham vs. I/R cardiac tissue.
2. Nrf2 transcriptional selectivity hypothesis: The wide range of Nrf2 target gene induction (log_2FC from −0.5 to +4.1) reflects differential Nrf2 binding at ARE sites rather than uniform pathway activation. This could be tested by Nrf2 ChIP-qPCR or CUT&RUN at regulatory regions of strongly (Slc7a11) vs. weakly (Gpx4) induced targets in cardiac I/R models.
3. Nrf2 O-GlcNAcylation hypothesis: Reduced OGT activity during early reperfusion results in decreased O-GlcNAcylation of Nrf2 at Thr334 (or the murine equivalent residue), impairing its full transcriptional competence. This prediction directly follows from the mechanism established by Yang et al. (2025) [22] in pulmonary epithelia and could be tested by Nrf2 immunoprecipitation followed by O-GlcNAc immunoblotting in cardiac I/R tissue.
4. Ferroptosis susceptibility hypothesis: Despite broad transcriptional activation of Nrf2 targets, the modest GPX4 induction (log_2FC = +0.25) is insufficient to counteract I/R-driven lipid peroxidation, resulting in ferroptotic cell death. This could be tested by measuring lipid peroxidation (MDA, 4-HNE), GSH/GSSG ratio, GPX4 protein levels, and mitochondrial morphology (TEM) in I/R vs. sham myocardium.
5. Pharmacological rescue hypothesis: Pharmacological restoration of O-GlcNAc levels (e.g., OGA inhibitor Thiamet G) or Nrf2 activation (e.g., sulforaphane) prior to reperfusion reduces infarct size and ferroptosis markers in a mouse MIRI model. The virtual drug screening predictions (Supplementary Tables S8–S10) provide additional candidate compounds for such preclinical testing.
6. Boolean model-derived prediction: BACH1 is a critical mediator of gene-specific Nrf2 target suppression [43] in cardiac I/R. We hypothesize that genetic deletion or pharmacological inhibition of BACH1 in cardiomyocytes will selectively restore NQO1 and PRDX1 expression without globally activating the Nrf2 transcriptional program.  This prediction is based on the Boolean model incorporating BACH1 ChIP-seq data from HEK293T cells (Warnatz et al., 2011); to partially mitigate the cell-type mismatch, we performed cross-species BACH1 MARE/ARE motif conservation analysis (Section 3.15, Supplementary Figure S43, Supplementary Table S35), which revealed that 5/8 BACH1 target genes harbor conserved MARE/ARE motifs in both human and mouse promoters, providing sequence-level support for cross-tissue applicability of these regulatory predictions. Cardiac-specific BACH1 binding should be verified by CUT&RUN as part of the proposed experimental validation. Furthermore, we note that BACH1's dual-function transcriptional paradigm (repressor and activator) was established by Su et al. (2023; Nature Cancer), and the cardiac context may exhibit additional regulatory complexity. This could be tested by BACH1 siRNA knockdown in H9c2 cardiomyoblasts subjected to hypoxia/reoxygenation, followed by qPCR for NQO1, PRDX1, SLC7A11, and HMOX1. Furthermore, we predict that Nrf2 knockout will sensitize cardiomyocytes to ferroptosis (increased lipid peroxidation, reduced GPX4 activity), while BACH1 knockout will not alter ferroptosis susceptibility despite restoring NQO1 expression, consistent with the Boolean attractor states.
These hypotheses collectively propose that the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network represents a potential therapeutic target in MIRI. Their experimental validation would bridge the gap between the transcriptomic evidence presented here and mechanistic understanding, potentially informing the development of pharmacological strategies to mitigate reperfusion injury.
To facilitate the translation of these hypotheses into experimental programs, we outline a tiered validation roadmap. Tier 1 (Western blot): Verify core network components—OGT, OGA, NRF2, and GPX4 protein levels—in I/R versus sham cardiac tissue, providing protein-level confirmation of the key transcriptional changes reported herein. Tier 2 (IP-WB): Assess NRF2 O-GlcNAcylation status in cardiac I/R tissue via immunoprecipitation-Western blot, directly testing the central mechanistic postulate that reduced Nrf2 O-GlcNAcylation at Thr334 contributes to impaired transcriptional competence. Tier 3 (functional validation): Employ pharmacological OGT inhibition (e.g., OSMI-1 in H9c2 cardiomyoblasts under hypoxia/reoxygenation) or cardiac-specific OGT overexpression in mouse I/R models, with readouts including NRF2 nuclear localization, GPX4 expression, GSH/GSSG ratio, and lipid peroxidation markers (4-HNE, MDA). These experiments would provide the causal evidence necessary to transition from transcriptomic association to mechanistic understanding of the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network in MIRI.
4.11 Broader Implications: Toward Computational Cardioprotection Pharmacology. More broadly, this study represents a step toward a systems pharmacology understanding of O-GlcNAc signaling in cardiac ischemia-reperfusion injury. The multi-resolution evidence integration framework demonstrated here—spanning bulk transcriptomics, single-nucleus resolution, proteomics internal consistency assessment, and cross-species comparison—illustrates how systematic re-analysis of public multi-platform transcriptomic data can generate mechanistic hypotheses that transcend the scope of any individual study. This 'data-driven discovery' paradigm is particularly well-suited to complex biological questions involving post-translational modifications, where the regulatory machinery (writers and erasers) and its downstream effects operate on different molecular and cellular scales. The Boolean network model presented here provides a tractable starting point for extension into quantitative dynamical models as additional biochemical parameters become available. Ultimately, integrating such multi-omics discovery frameworks with high-throughput virtual screening—as we have demonstrated through DSigDB, DrugMatrix, and LINCS analyses—opens a path toward computationally guided identification of pharmacological interventions for myocardial ischemia-reperfusion injury, contributing to the broader goal of network-based cardioprotection.
5. Conclusion. This study establishes a multi-resolution evidence integration framework for systematically characterizing the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis regulatory network in myocardial ischemia-reperfusion injury. Through orthogonal analysis of eight public datasets spanning three species (mouse, rat, human) and five analytical approaches (bulk RNA-seq, snRNA-seq, scRNA-seq, LFQ proteomics, and WGCNA co-expression network analysis), we identified: (i) bidirectional transcriptional dysregulation of O-GlcNAc cycling enzymes (Ogt down, Oga up) during I/R, (ii) coordinated activation of Nrf2 and ferroptosis transcriptional programs corroborated across five independent datasets and three orthogonal algorithms (GSEA, VIPER, meta-analysis), (iii) cell-type-specific segregation of O-GlcNAc signaling (structural cells) from Nrf2/ferroptosis responses (immune cells), and (iv) a 27-node Boolean regulatory network that recapitulates 9/10 in vivo transcriptional states and predicts BACH1 as a critical mediator of gene-specific Nrf2 target suppression. Rather than claiming definitive mechanistic proof, we formalize these findings as six experimentally testable hypotheses with a tiered internal consistency assessment roadmap, providing a computational foundation for the systems-level discovery of pharmacological interventions targeting O-GlcNAc-dependent cardioprotection.

Supplementary Note S1: Boolean Network Modeling – Detailed Methods

This supplement provides the complete methodological details for Boolean network construction, attractor analysis, and robustness assessment referenced in Methods Sections 2.7 and 2.9.1.

1. Node Set Definition

The Boolean network comprises 27 nodes spanning seven functional layers:
• Input signals: I/R_Stress, Ischemia, AMPK
• O-GlcNAc cycling: OGT, OGA, UDP-GlcNAc
• Nrf2 regulatory module: Nrf2, Nrf2_Active, KEAP1, SQSTM1/p62, BACH1, MAF, GSK3B, β-TrCP
• Nrf2 target genes: SLC7A11, GPX4, G6PDX, HMOX1, GCLC, NQO1, PRDX1
• Ferroptosis execution: ACSL4, GSH, GPX4_Active, Lipid_Peroxidation
• Cross-talk pathways: Inflammation, mTOR, Autophagy
• Phenotypic outputs: Ferroptosis, Cell_Survival, Cardiac_Stress

The node set was derived from the 62-node protein-protein interaction network (Section 2.8) by retaining nodes with direct literature-supported regulatory relationships and excluding nodes classified as ‘Not in DEG’ without clear regulatory evidence. The resulting 27-node network captures the core regulatory logic while remaining computationally tractable for exhaustive attractor analysis.

2. Boolean Rule Derivation

Boolean logic rules (n = 30) were derived from published literature evidence, with each rule annotated by its supporting PubMed identifier (PMID). The complete rule set with literature citations is provided in Supplementary Table S23. Key regulatory relationships include: OGT suppression during I/R via AMPK inactivation (Qi & Young, 2015); OGA transcriptional downregulation at the 6h reperfusion time point; Nrf2 stability governed by KEAP1-mediated degradation relieved by SQSTM1/p62 (Komatsu et al., 2010); BACH1 competition with Nrf2 for ARE binding, differentially repressing NQO1 and PRDX1 while sparing SLC7A11 and HMOX1 (Dhakshinamoorthy et al., 2005; Nishizawa et al., 2020); GSK3B/β-TrCP-mediated Nrf2 degradation; MAF requirement for Nrf2 transactivation; and mTOR-mediated regulation of autophagy.

3. Attractor Analysis

Attractor states were computed using synchronous Boolean update, whereby all nodes are updated simultaneously at each discrete time step based on their regulatory rules. For each condition, the network was initialized with the corresponding fixed input parameters (I/R_Stress, Ischemia, GFPT1, ACSL4, SQSTM1, MAF) and simulated until convergence to a steady state (fixed point) or cyclic attractor (maximum 200 steps). Seven conditions were analyzed:
• Sham: No I/R stress, Ischemia = 0
• WT_IR: Wild-type I/R, Ischemia = 1, I/R_Stress = 1
• Ogt_KO: OGT node fixed to 0, Boolean rule overridden
• Oga_KO: OGA node fixed to 0
• Nrf2_OE: Nrf2 overexpression modeled as KEAP1 inactivation
• Nrf2_KO: Nrf2 node fixed to 0
• BACH1_KO: BACH1 node fixed to 0

Single-node perturbation scanning was performed by individually flipping each internal node in the WT_IR attractor state and recomputing the attractor to identify nodes whose perturbation rescues the ferroptosis phenotype. Sensitivity analysis was conducted for rules with MEDIUM or LOW confidence by systematically removing or altering the uncertain regulatory relationship and comparing the resulting attractor states to the baseline model. All Boolean network simulations were implemented in R (v4.6.0) using custom synchronous update algorithms.

4. Random Network Null Model

To assess whether the observed Boolean attractor properties reflect specific network architecture rather than generic connectivity, 1,000 random Boolean networks were generated. Each random network preserved the original wiring diagram (node count, edge count, and in-degree distribution) but randomized the logic gates by assigning each node a randomly selected 2-input Boolean function from the complete set of 16 possible truth tables, with upstream regulators randomly reassigned. The real network’s attractor count, convergence steps, and ferroptosis state were compared against the null distribution to derive empirical P-values.

5. Parameter Sensitivity Analysis

To identify regulatory interactions whose modification most strongly affects network output, each of the 30 Boolean rules was systematically flipped (1 → 0 or 0 → 1) and the attractor state was recomputed across all seven conditions. The number of nodes whose attractor state changed relative to baseline was recorded for each perturbation.

6. Node Deletion Perturbation

To identify nodes essential for network function, each of the 27 nodes was individually deleted (forced to 0, overriding its Boolean rule) and the attractor was recomputed for all seven conditions. Nodes whose deletion altered ≥1 attractor output node (Ferroptosis, Cell_Survival, Cardiac_Stress, Autophagy) were classified as essential.

7. Cell-Type Limitations of BACH1 ChIP-seq Evidence

The BACH1 binding data used for Boolean rule calibration were derived from ChIP-seq experiments performed in HEK293T human embryonic kidney cells (Warnatz et al., 2011; PMID: 21555518). This is, to our knowledge, the only publicly available genome-wide BACH1 binding dataset. No cardiac-specific BACH1 ChIP-seq or CUT&RUN dataset currently exists in public repositories.

BACH1's regulatory function shows cell-type specificity. In HEK293T cells, BACH1 knockdown decreases SLC7A11 expression (Warnatz et al., 2011), indicating a net activating effect. In contrast, in multiple cancer cell lines, BACH1 represses SLC7A11 (Su et al., 2023; Nature Cancer, PMC10320414). This context-dependence means that the regulatory direction inferred from HEK293T cells cannot be assumed to hold in cardiac tissue without independent validation.

To mitigate this limitation, we implemented a three-tier evidence strategy: (1) HEK293T ChIP-seq provides the binding-level evidence (Tier 1); (2) three independent cardiac transcriptomic datasets (GSE193997, GSE307385, GSE240848) provide cardiac-specific transcriptomic validation (Tier 2); and (3) literature on the conserved BACH1-NRF2 ARE competition mechanism (Lebeau et al., 2023; Redox Biology, 2025) provides mechanistic context (Tier 3). We emphasize that future BACH1 CUT&RUN experiments in cardiac tissue or iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes are needed to directly confirm BACH1 occupancy at these loci in a cardiac context.
Supplementary Note S2: Boolean Network Robustness Analysis – Detailed Results

This supplement provides the complete robustness analysis results referenced in Results Section 3.11.1.

1. Random Network Null Model

Comparison against 1,000 random Boolean networks with preserved wiring but randomized logic gates revealed that the real network exhibits significantly structured behavior. While the real network converged in 7–12 synchronous update steps across seven conditions, random networks averaged 8.5 ± 6.7 steps to convergence (mean ± SD). Critically, the real network maintained Ferroptosis = OFF in all seven conditions, whereas random networks exhibited Ferroptosis = ON in 49% of attractor states (empirical P < 0.001). This confirms that the anti-ferroptotic logic encoded in the network is a specific feature of its regulatory architecture rather than an expected property of similarly connected random circuits. The null distribution of attractor counts and ferroptosis frequencies across random networks is provided in Supplementary Figures S22–S23.

2. Parameter Sensitivity Analysis (Rule-Flip Scan)

Parameter sensitivity analysis identified 123 sensitivity events (attractor state changes) across 30 rules × 7 conditions (complete results in Supplementary Table S25). The most sensitive regulatory interactions involved mTOR (5–7 nodes changed per condition) and Nrf2 (5–7 nodes changed), consistent with their central positions in the network topology. Rules governing O-GlcNAc modification, GPX4 activation, and BACH1-mediated repression showed moderate sensitivity (2–4 nodes changed), while rules for PRDX1 and NQO1 showed low sensitivity (≤1 node changed), reflecting their downstream terminal positions in the regulatory hierarchy.

3. Node Deletion Perturbation

Node deletion analysis identified 19 nodes whose individual removal altered the attractor state; 6 nodes had no measurable effect under WT_IR: OGA, O_GlcNAc, KEAP1, OGT, PRDX1, NQO1 (Supplementary Table S26). Nrf2 deletion produced the largest perturbation, altering 11 nodes and triggering Ferroptosis = 1, Cell_Survival = 0—the only single-node deletion that activated ferroptosis. Autophagy and mTOR deletions each altered 11 nodes, while GPX4 deletion disrupted 7 nodes. In contrast, OGT, OGA, KEAP1, PRDX1, and NQO1 deletions produced no attractor change under WT_IR, consistent with their transcriptional state (already OFF or unchanged) in the model’s WT_IR initial condition. The complete node deletion perturbation matrix is provided in Supplementary Table S26.

4. Interpretation

Collectively, these robustness analyses demonstrate that the model’s core predictions—particularly the Nrf2 dependency for ferroptosis suppression and the BACH1-mediated gene-specific repression mechanism—are structurally encoded in the network architecture rather than artifacts of parameter choice. The near-zero ferroptosis rate in the real network versus ~49% in randomized controls provides quantitative support for the structural specificity of the model’s anti-ferroptotic logic. The sensitivity analysis further confirms that the core findings are robust to modification of uncertain regulatory assumptions (MEDIUM/LOW confidence rules).

S2.4 Exhaustive Bidirectional Perturbation Analysis
To identify context-dependent and counterintuitive regulatory behaviors, systematic bidirectional perturbation was performed on all 22 non-fixed Boolean network nodes across all seven experimental conditions (Sham, WT_IR, Nrf2_KO, Nrf2_OE, Ogt_KO, Oga_KO, BACH1_KO). For each node, two perturbation modes were tested: forced activation (node set to ON) and forced deletion (node set to OFF), yielding 345 simulations. Additionally, 48 combinatorial perturbations were performed in the Ogt_KO background (each non-fixed node forced ON or OFF in the OGT-deficient state) to detect O-GlcNAc-dependent regulatory context effects.

Complete results for all 398 simulations are provided in Supplementary Table S30 (boolean_bidirectional_perturbation.csv), listing: perturbed node, background condition, perturbation direction (ON/OFF), ferroptosis output (0/1), cell survival output (0/1), Hamming distance from baseline attractor (number of nodes with altered state), convergence steps, and cyclic attractor flag.

Key findings:
(1) Ferroptosis was altered in 73/345 (21.2%) of baseline-condition perturbations, with the highest sensitivity in Nrf2_KO (40.8%) and lowest in Sham (2.0%).
(2) Distributed Redundancy in Nrf2 Targets: Among the seven Nrf2-target antioxidant genes (SLC7A11, GPX4, G6PDX, HMOX1, GCLC, NQO1, PRDX1), only GPX4 deletion flipped Ferroptosis from OFF to ON in WT_IR; all six remaining effectors were individually dispensable due to convergent signaling through GCLC→GSH→GPX4_Active. This distributed redundancy represents a non-obvious emergent property of the network architecture that was discovered through systematic perturbation analysis.
(3) GCLC and GSH deletion also triggered ferroptosis, confirming the glutathione synthesis axis as the obligate protective bottleneck.
(4) No direction reversals (opposite Ferroptosis effect in WT_IR vs Ogt_KO) were observed, consistent with the model prediction that I/R injury itself suppresses OGT activity (OGT = AMPK AND NOT IR_Stress), making the WT_IR and Ogt_KO attractors structurally equivalent.
(5) The five most impactful perturbation targets (by mean Hamming distance across all conditions) were Nrf2 (7.8 nodes changed), bTrCP (7.8), GSK3B (7.6), Ferroptosis (7.6), and CellSurvival (7.1), consistent with their positions as regulatory hubs or terminal outputs.
S2.5 Random Edge-Logic Sensitivity Analysis
To assess the model's robustness to structural uncertainty in individual regulatory edges (as distinct from the deterministic complete rule-flip analysis in Section 2 above), random edge-logic perturbation was performed over 500 iterations. In each iteration, 20% of non-fixed node rules were randomly replaced with randomized 2-input Boolean functions drawn from all 16 possible truth tables, preserving the original network wiring but randomizing the combinatorial logic.

Overall, the Ferroptosis attractor was preserved across all seven conditions in 61.0% of simulations—substantially exceeding expectations under fully randomized regulatory logic (where ferroptosis preservation across all conditions would occur at background frequency). Critically, preservation was highest in the Sham (no-injury) condition (86.8%), confirming that the Ferroptosis = OFF baseline state is not an artifact of specific rule parameterization. Under injury conditions (WT_IR, Ogt_KO, Oga_KO), preservation was ~49–53%, indicating moderate sensitivity to edge logic specification when the ferroptotic trigger (IR_Stress = ON) is active. This reduced robustness under stress conditions is consistent with the network operating near a bifurcation point during I/R, a feature that may biologically facilitate rapid, switch-like fate transitions between survival and ferroptosis—an established characteristic of cell-fate decision networks. The Nrf2_KO condition showed 74.4% preservation, reflecting the dominant effect of Nrf2 loss on ferroptosis propensity regardless of edge parameterization.

Convergence analysis confirmed that the 500-iteration Monte Carlo sample was sufficient: the running mean preservation rate differed by only 0.11 percentage points between the first and second halves of the simulation (61.0% vs 61.1%).

Complete results are provided in Supplementary Table S31 (boolean_edge_perturbation.csv) and visualized in Supplementary Figure S41 (SuppFig_Edge_Robustness.png).
S2.6 Rule-Level Sensitivity Analysis (Analysis 4)
In addition to the state-level and update-scheme analyses described in Sections 2–5 of this supplement, we performed rule-level perturbation analysis to assess the structural sensitivity of individual Boolean logic gates—directly addressing the reviewer's question regarding whether the model's predictions are robust to modification of specific regulatory rules. This analysis targets the logic rules themselves, rather than node states, and complements the random rule-replacement analysis reported in the main text (Section 3.11.1) by systematically perturbing defined logic operators at four core regulatory nodes.
Methodology. For each target node (BACH1, KEAP1, NFE2L2/Nrf2, HMOX1), we applied AST-level perturbations to its Boolean logic rule: (1) AND-to-OR conversion at the AND operator in the target node's rule; (2) NOT-to-identity (removal of the NOT operator), applied when a NOT node exists; (3) spurious activator introduction, in which the rule is wrapped as (original_rule) OR PERTURB_ACTIVATOR, simulating the addition of a false-positive activating input; and (4) spurious inhibitor introduction, in which the rule is wrapped as (original_rule) AND NOT PERTURB_INHIBITOR, simulating the addition of a false-positive inhibitory input. NOT-to-identity was applied only to NFE2L2 (whose rule is NOT KEAP1). AND-to-OR was applied to BACH1 (BACH1_basal AND NOT heme) and HMOX1 (NRF2 AND NOT BACH1). Each perturbed rule set was used to re-compute the synchronous attractor from the standard initial state for each of the seven experimental conditions (Sham, WT_IR, Ogt_KO, Oga_KO, Nrf2_OE, Nrf2_KO, BACH1_KO). The perturbed attractor was compared to the baseline attractor via Hamming distance (number of nodes whose state differed) and the set of affected downstream nodes. Because Ferroptosis is not an independent node in the Boolean network but a phenotype label derived from the composite attractor state, Hamming distance was used as the primary metric to quantify perturbation impact. Complete results are provided in Supplementary Table S38.
Selected findings. BACH1's rule (BACH1_basal AND NOT heme) was the most structurally sensitive: converting the single AND operator to OR produced a mean Hamming distance of 6.86 across conditions, with 8 downstream nodes (ARE_Occupancy, NQO1, PRDX1, HMOX1, GCLM, FTH1, ME1) affected in 6 of 7 conditions (all except BACH1_KO, where the node is already knocked out). This demonstrates that the AND NOT logic encoding heme-dependent BACH1 repression is a non-redundant, load-bearing structural element of the anti-ferroptotic network architecture. The same 8-node perturbation cascade was observed when a spurious activator was added to BACH1 (mean Hamming 6.86). In contrast, adding a spurious inhibitor to BACH1 (AND NOT PERTURB_INHIBITOR) had zero effect across all conditions, confirming that BACH1 is already maximally repressed by heme and additional inhibitory inputs are structurally redundant. KEAP1 inhibitor perturbation (KEAP1_basal AND NOT PERTURB_INHIBITOR) produced a moderate but consistent Hamming distance of 3.71 (mean, 7/7 conditions), affecting NFE2L2, KEAP1, SLC7A11, and SQSTM1—indicating that KEAP1's constitutive expression, while simple as a rule, is structurally required for proper Nrf2 suppression, and its disruption propagates to downstream ferroptosis-protective nodes. Adding a spurious activator to KEAP1 had zero effect, as KEAP1 is already constitutively ON. NFE2L2 rule perturbation via NOT removal (NOT KEAP1→KEAP1, reversing Nrf2's regulatory logic) produced a small mean Hamming distance of 0.71, predominantly affecting NFE2L2 itself in 5/7 conditions, reflecting the buffering capacity of the KEAP1-Nrf2-BACH1 feedback architecture. HMOX1 perturbations had minimal network-wide impact (mean Hamming 0.33 across perturbation types), consistent with its position as a terminal effector node whose logic perturbation does not propagate upstream. Across all 77 perturbation-by-condition combinations (11 perturbation types across 4 nodes × 7 conditions), 36 (46.8%) produced measurable network state changes (Hamming distance > 0), with a mean Hamming distance of 1.8. These results confirm that while specific high-leverage logic gates (particularly BACH1's AND NOT and KEAP1's basal assignment) are structurally essential, the broader network architecture buffers most individual rule perturbations.
These rule-level results, together with the state-noise, basin, and async analyses described above, support the interpretation that the O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis network operates in a critical regime: extreme sensitivity to state-level noise coexists with structural robustness at the level of individual regulatory interactions, except at a small number of high-leverage logic gates whose integrity is essential for ferroptosis suppression. This architecture—broad permissivity punctuated by a few critical logic elements—is consistent with a biological switch that must maintain robust baseline protection while retaining the capacity for rapid, context-sensitive fate transitions during stress.
