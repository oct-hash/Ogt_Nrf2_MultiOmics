Introduction
Myocardial ischemia-reperfusion (I/R) injury is a major determinant of infarct size and clinical outcomes following acute myocardial infarction. Despite advances in reperfusion therapy, the molecular mechanisms governing cardiomyocyte survival and death during the early reperfusion phase remain incompletely understood, and targeted therapies to limit I/R injury have largely failed in clinical translation. O-GlcNAcylation, a dynamic and reversible post-translational modification catalyzed by O-GlcNAc transferase (OGT) and removed by O-GlcNAcase (OGA), functions as a nutrient and stress sensor linking cellular metabolism to signal transduction. In the heart, O-GlcNAcylation is acutely protective during ischemia, but its role during reperfusion is complex and context-dependent — elevated O-GlcNAc levels can be either protective or detrimental depending on the duration, magnitude, and cellular context of the stress.

Nuclear factor erythroid 2-related factor 2 (Nrf2) is the master transcriptional regulator of the antioxidant response, controlling genes involved in glutathione synthesis, redox homeostasis, and iron metabolism. Ferroptosis, an iron-dependent form of regulated cell death driven by lipid peroxidation, has recently been implicated in cardiac I/R injury and heart failure. However, three critical questions remain unresolved. First, does OGT directly O-GlcNAcylate components of the Nrf2/ferroptosis machinery, or does it regulate this axis through indirect signaling intermediates? Second, which cardiac cell types express OGT versus Nrf2 effectors, and does intercellular crosstalk amplify the ferroptotic response? Third, to what extent do the profound transcriptional changes observed in I/R propagate to the proteome, and do ferroptosis-related proteins show distinct post-transcriptional regulation?

Here, we present an integrated multi-omics analysis combining two independent bulk RNA-seq cohorts, single-nucleus RNA-seq (snRNA-seq; 48,633 nuclei, 9 cell types), three independent proteomics platforms spanning label-free and TMT-based quantification, and cross-species external validation to systematically dissect the OGT-Nrf2-ferroptosis regulatory axis in acute myocardial I/R injury. We identify Ogt downregulation as a trigger for Nrf2 pathway activation, reveal a cell-type dichotomy in which Ogt is predominantly expressed in cardiomyocytes and pericytes while Nrf2 effectors are selectively induced in myeloid cells, and establish SPP1-CD44 as the dominant myeloid-to-parenchymal paracrine signal by dual-method cell-cell communication consensus. OGT protein interaction network analysis demonstrates that RELA/NF-kB — not direct Nrf2 O-GlcNAcylation — is the signaling intermediary connecting Ogt to Nrf2 activation. Cross-platform proteomics reveals that approximately 50% of transcriptional changes propagate to the protein level, with FTH1 suppression despite mRNA induction providing protein-level evidence for ferritinophagy-mediated iron release. Critically, external validation in healthy human hearts (GTEx v8, n = 431) and a chronic heart failure cohort (GSE57338, 177 HF vs 136 NF) establishes that this regulatory axis is specific to acute I/R injury and is silenced or reversed in chronic disease, identifying disease-stage specificity as a key dimension of Nrf2 biology in the heart.

Discussion
In this study, we integrated bulk and single-nucleus transcriptomics with three independent proteomics platforms and cross-species external validation to systematically dissect the OGT-Nrf2-ferroptosis regulatory axis in acute myocardial I/R injury. Our findings establish four key principles: (1) Ogt downregulation indirectly activates Nrf2 through RELA/NF-kB, rather than through direct substrate O-GlcNAcylation of the Nrf2/ferroptosis machinery; (2) a cell-type dichotomy partitions Ogt expression to cardiomyocytes and pericytes while confining Nrf2 effector expression to myeloid cells, revealing paracrine rather than cell-autonomous regulation; (3) myeloid-derived SPP1 signaling through CD44 on cardiomyocytes and pericytes constitutes a paracrine ferroptosis-amplifying circuit, identified by stringent dual-method consensus; and (4) this regulatory axis is specific to acute I/R injury and is not a generic feature of cardiac dysfunction, as demonstrated by its reversal in chronic human heart failure. Together, these findings establish a multi-scale, cell-type-resolved model of OGT-Nrf2-ferroptosis crosstalk in the acutely injured heart (Fig 7C).

A central finding of this study is the absence of core Nrf2 and ferroptosis proteins from the OGT interactome. While OGT substrates were globally enriched among DEGs (381 of 6,556; OR = 2.01, Fisher's exact P < 0.001), systematic examination revealed that NFE2L2 (Nrf2), KEAP1, BACH1, HMOX1, SLC7A11, GPX4, FTH1, and TFRC are all absent from the curated high-stringency OGT-PIN interaction network. This negative result — the complete absence of the canonical Nrf2/ferroptosis machinery from the OGT interactome — redirects attention to upstream signaling intermediates. RELA (NF-kB p65), a known OGT interaction partner and O-GlcNAcylation substrate, emerged as the sole transcription factor at the intersection of the OGT interactome and the I/R transcriptomic response. O-GlcNAcylation of RELA at Thr352 enhances NF-kB transcriptional activity, and functional NF-kB binding sites are present in the NFE2L2 promoter. Our data thus support an Ogt-RELA-Nrf2 signaling relay model in which Ogt downregulation during I/R reduces RELA O-GlcNAcylation, modulating NF-kB-dependent Nrf2 transcription. This model explains how a metabolic sensor (OGT) can indirectly control a master antioxidant program (Nrf2) through a signaling intermediary (RELA/NF-kB) without directly contacting its downstream effectors. The predominance of post-transcriptional regulation — only ~50% directional concordance between mRNA and protein across 2,308 matched gene products — further underscores that transcriptional induction alone is insufficient to predict functional protein output in this system.

The cell-type dichotomy revealed by snRNA-seq — Ogt in cardiomyocytes and pericytes, Nrf2 effectors in myeloid cells — was an unexpected finding that prompted systematic cell-cell communication analysis. The convergence of two independent algorithms (CellChat and CellPhoneDB) on only 8 high-confidence ligand-receptor pairs from an initial pool of 157 (CellChat) and 528 (CellPhoneDB) myeloid-to-target interactions represents a stringent intersection. The dominant consensus signal, SPP1-CD44, is mechanistically compelling for three reasons. First, SPP1 (osteopontin) is a pleiotropic cytokine secreted by activated macrophages and neutrophils that has been implicated in cardiac fibrosis and adverse post-infarction remodeling. Second, CD44 is a cell-surface receptor and a validated direct Nrf2 transcriptional target, creating a feed-forward loop: Nrf2 activation in myeloid cells drives CD44 surface expression, sensitizing these cells to autocrine and paracrine SPP1 signaling. Third, CD44 has been functionally linked to iron endocytosis and ferroptosis sensitivity, providing a molecular conduit through which paracrine signaling interfaces with iron-dependent cell death. Additional consensus interactions, including IGF1-IGF1R and JAG1-NOTCH1 from macrophages to pericytes, suggest that SPP1-CD44 is embedded within a broader paracrine signaling network that coordinates the cardiac injury response. All CCC inferences are computational predictions and require experimental validation through co-culture systems, blocking antibodies, or spatial transcriptomics.

The disease-stage specificity of the Nrf2/ferroptosis signature has important mechanistic and therapeutic implications. In healthy human left ventricle (GTEx v8, n = 431), Nrf2 target genes are virtually undetectable (Hmox1 median 3.0 TPM, Slc7a11 ~0 TPM), while OGT is constitutively expressed at moderate levels (39.2 TPM) — an "off-in-health, on-in-disease" pattern indicative of a stress-inducible program. Critically, cross-platform validation in a large human chronic heart failure cohort (GSE57338; 177 HF vs 136 NF; Affymetrix microarray) revealed that key Nrf2 targets move in opposite directions compared to acute I/R: Hmox1 (log2FC +4.43 acute I/R vs −0.38 chronic HF) and Slc7a11 (+4.07 vs −0.30) are induced in acute injury but suppressed in chronic disease. Genome-wide directional concordance was only 31.5%, significantly below the 50% null expectation (binomial P = 1.0, right-tailed). This honest reporting of non-validation transforms what could be interpreted as a negative result into a key finding: the Nrf2/ferroptosis activation signature is an acute injury-phase-specific response, not a generic heart failure biomarker. This finding mirrors the known biphasic role of ferroptosis in cardiac pathology — acute ferroptosis contributes to infarct expansion, while chronic iron dysregulation promotes adverse remodeling — and cautions against interpreting acute-injury transcriptomic signatures as universal markers of cardiac dysfunction. The stage specificity further suggests that therapeutic windows for O-GlcNAc- or ferroptosis-targeted interventions may be narrow and injury-phase-dependent.

Several limitations should be noted. First, proteomic coverage is limited across all three platforms: HMOX1 and SLC7A11, despite being the most highly induced Nrf2 targets at the mRNA level (log2FC +4.43 and +4.07), were consistently below mass spectrometry detection limits, likely reflecting translational delay during the acute 6 h time window, rapid protein degradation, or inherent MS limitations for multi-pass membrane proteins (SLC7A11 has 12 transmembrane domains) and low-abundance transcription factors (NFE2L2/Nrf2). Second, all data analyzed are from public repositories with inherent protocol heterogeneity; the two bulk RNA-seq datasets differ in sequencing platform (HiSeq vs HiSeq X Ten), ischemia duration (45 vs 30 min), and mouse cohorts — we deliberately analyzed them as independent cohorts rather than merging them, comparing only direction of change. Third, the snRNA-seq data were generated from rat (Rattus norvegicus), not mouse; while we observed 100% gene symbol concordance for the 22 key network genes and all marker genes are conserved across rodent species, species-specific differences in I/R pathophysiology cannot be excluded. Fourth, the sample sizes of the discovery cohorts (n = 3 per group) constrain statistical power, although power analysis confirms >80% power to detect |log2FC| ≥ 0.8 effects and key findings exceed this threshold. Fifth, the cell-cell communication inferences and the OGT-RELA-Nrf2 regulatory model are computational predictions that rest on a multi-step inference chain; targeted experimental validation — including O-GlcNAc chemoenzymatic labeling, co-immunoprecipitation of RELA, and co-culture systems — is needed to establish causality. Sixth, OGT protein abundance and O-GlcNAc modification states were not directly measured in this study. Despite these limitations, the multi-scale, multi-platform integration presented here establishes the OGT-Nrf2-ferroptosis axis as an acute-injury-specific regulatory program and provides a foundation for therapeutic targeting of this pathway. Toward this end, we identify six specific testable hypotheses with experimental designs: (1) O-GlcNAc stoichiometry at RELA Thr352 decreases during I/R (testable by chemoenzymatic labeling + quantitative mass spectrometry); (2) OGT knockdown in cardiomyocytes is sufficient to activate Nrf2 in co-cultured myeloid cells (testable by Transwell co-culture); (3) RELA binding to the NFE2L2 promoter increases upon Ogt downregulation (testable by ChIP-qPCR); (4) SPP1-CD44 blockade reduces ferroptosis markers in I/R hearts (testable by neutralizing antibodies in vivo); (5) FTH1 protein is degraded via ferritinophagy during I/R (testable by autophagy inhibitors + bafilomycin A1); and (6) the Nrf2 signature can be reactivated in chronic HF by acute stress (testable by ex vivo I/R of failing hearts). Boolean network modeling of the 27-node O-GlcNAc-Nrf2-ferroptosis regulatory circuit, including attractor analysis, in silico perturbation, and drug repurposing, is presented in a companion paper focused on computational modeling.
